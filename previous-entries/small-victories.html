There's a Reason Most People Don't Do This »

India is an incredible country, a nation so many people before me have described as the land of juxtapositions – grieving poverty next to astounding luxury, brightly clad people walking through brown dirty streets, urine smelling rickshaw stands in front of ornate centuries-old mausoleums, and shameless swindlers mixed among honest merchants.  All of these contrasts and more had been described to me before ever I set foot in India through either first hand accounts, second hand generalizations, or stories I’d read in books or photos I’d seen in National Geographic magazines.  I didn’t even wish to visit India when I was younger precisely because I knew it could be such a difficult trip, but having now visited over twenty countries in my life, many of them outside the West, I thought that I would be ready for India and that the culture shock most westerners feel when coming here wouldn’t hit me so hard.  That was definitely not true.

At least for me, it is not the poverty – the one aspect of India cited more than any other as the most difficult to stomach – that got to me, for I have seen poverty before.  Rather, it was the inescapable crowdedness coupled with a model of efficiency (or lack thereof) that relies entirely upon personal contacts and referrals that drove me nearly crazy during my first week here as I attempted to commence my motorcycle tour with my best friend Nick.  The relatively simple tasks (by American standards) of renting two motorcycles, driving them along a major highway to and from a city less than two hundred miles away, and then finding a hotel where we’d booked a room within a major metropolis pushed Nick and me to our very limits.  But this is the story of what happened next, because from every challenge comes a lesson, and from every struggle comes wisdom, and since leaving Delhi with our duly earned wisdom Nick and I have been achieving, on a relatively consistent basis, not only our first cardinal rule of world traveling: Don’t Die, but also our second: Have Fun.

The key to this newfound enjoyment has in large part been due to recalibrating of expectations.  Throw out entirely what you think is possible or not possible based on previous experiences in life, and instead rely only on experiences so far in India.  What this means is that eating a delicious and filling meal from a dirty looking roadside restaurant is possible, but expecting a warm shower in even a modern looking hotel is not possible.  This means using your cell phone to enlist the help of a man whom you know only by a business card and a referral is possible, but finding an address without making several wrong turns and asking upwards of half a dozen people for directions is not possible.  This means walking away from any business, be it a street vendor or a fancy restaurant, to secure a fairer price is possible, but stopping two luggage-laden motorcycles anywhere on the side of the highway without attracting a large crowd is not possible.  And if you have no experience to go on, expect the unexpected, because that’s almost always what you’re going to get.

The beginning of our real India motorcycle tour, the one whose route I will someday trace on a map of India with large westerly and southerly strokes, began four days ago in Varanasi, arguably the holiest city in Hinduism with its iconic steps and waterfront temples on the banks of the Ganges River.  To get there we took the overnight train on which we also booked passage for our motorcycles in the freight car.  The train was due to arrive at 7:30 the next morning, and after working all day to frantically wrap up our affairs in Delhi (where Nick and his traveling companion Sally had been living for over two months and I had been attempting to buy an elusive GPS navigation system) and successfully get our bikes onboard the correct train, we sat back in our first class sleeper car as it rolled away from the station in wonder that we had actually accomplished everything. 

Everything, that is, but buying a few celebratory drinks for the all night journey.  Why had we forgotten to do that? Nick, Sally and I asked out loud with sheepish grins on our faces.  Not to sound like alcoholics, but we all agreed that beer and liquor were invented for times like these.  A few minutes later our cheerful train steward poked his head into our compartment and asked us if we’d like anything to drink.  “Beer?” we asked enthusiastically all the while knowing his response would certainly be no.  We were correct – there was no beer onboard.  “Ohh…” we joked with him, “We’d do almost anything for a few beers right now.”  “Nothing at all,” we asked again, “no alcohol onboard at all?”  “We’ll make it well worth your while,” we assured him.  Then, with a quick glance over his shoulder and an instant change of facial expression from jovial to serious he pulled the curtain of our compartment closed and leaned in to say softly, “Whisky?”  “YES!!!” we almost exploded with excitement!  Shh!!!  He implored us to be quiet, looking nervous and dead serious.  He made some hand gestures that we took to mean he’d be back later.  Was this really happening?  We were really about to buy illicit alcohol onboard an Indian train? 

Even though the alcohol we did buy (brought to us wrapped in a plain white towel) lasted less than half an hour and amounted to only a shot or two each (well… actually more for me, because I greedily filled my half-emptied Pepsi bottle nearly to the top thinking our repeat order would materialize, although it never did), the moral victory was clear: we’d accomplished something we didn’t think we could in India, doing it the Indian way, and yes, we even have the picture to prove it. 

The next day we arrived promptly at noon in Varanasi, and waiting for us there was an employee of the guesthouse we’d booked a room in at the recommendation of some friends at my sister’s wedding reception a few days before.  This made all the difference as he helped us to navigate the tedious and ripe-for-extortion set of wickets required to unload our bikes from the train’s freight car and release them from the freight terminal back onto the street.  As we stood in front of the train station and completed our ten-minute-long ritual of tying our various pieces of luggage to our steel luggage racks such that the bumps and vibrations of driving wouldn’t knock them loose, a crowd we counted to be over seventy onlookers surrounded us, one so determined to see what was going on that he climbed on top of a traffic pylon to get a better vantage.  A few minutes later we were off, our new escort riding back seat on my motorcycle and guiding us through the teaming streets directly to the guesthouse.  Oh what a difference from a few days ago when we tried the same thing on our own in Delhi.  It was another small victory, but a victory nonetheless. 

That evening we took a nighttime stroll along the waterfront steps of Varanasi, and as the day’s main energies of pilgrimage and tourism had already been exhausted, we actually enjoyed a some quiet moments to take in the sights of this ancient city built literally to the edge of this holy river.  

The next day I ventured out on my own and, between finding a mechanic to fix the handlebars on my motorcycle that had been bent in the overstuffed freight car and finding an electrician to fit the bike with a cigarette lighter socket through which to power my MapMyIndia GPS console, I took a boat ride along the Ganges, saw where the dead are washed and cremated at the river’s edge, and even visited a Hindu temple buried deep within the maze of narrow alleyways that separate the buildings of the old city adjacent to the river.  All of this was due to befriending a man of about my age who I originally took to be just another shop solicitor, but who in the end became my personal tour guide of the old city I would have been completely lost in by myself.  Nick and Sally reported similar good fortune for their day’s outing.  So despite our first impressions having been that Varanasi was another crowded, dirty, and traffic clogged city, our opinion leaving it the next day was markedly improved. 

Our good fortune seemed to continue over the next three days, and each day, while certainly far from trouble free, continued the positive trend of less frustration and more enjoyment, and coupled with this was an ever-improving calibration of what is possible and what is not possible in India.  To me a small victory became discovering that the loss of my motorcycle’s entire electrical system was due simply to a blown fuse instead of a more complicated problem that would extend it’s inability to start into the indeterminate future.  A small victory was finding a delicious meal at a roadside shack restaurant where the staff and local patrons actually left us in peace and didn’t crowd around like paparazzi to tabloid celebrities.  A small victory was even finding a hotel room for the night (albeit, a pretty nasty one) only a kilometer after leaving the highway at dusk in Kanpur a full half hour before a power outage turned the whole town dark. 

Yesterday evening we arrived in the city of Agra after a full day’s ride for our quick one night stopover to see the Taj Mahal this morning before continuing on to the much more interesting (according to conventional tourist wisdom) city of Jaipur, but to our disappointment we soon after learned that the Taj Mahal is closed on Fridays and that in order to see the inside we would have to stay an additional night in Agra.  Well, this little kink in our plans turned into a real blessing, because not only did we get to take a much desired day off after two long days on the road, we actually all had a great day in Agra, a city that despite its reputation as a shameless tourist trap offered friendly service, beautiful sightseeing, and even a smog-free sunny day, the likes of which I had yet to see in any Indian city.  And to finish off the day, at this very moment I am enjoying a Kingfisher beer and the sound of Italian opera in the lobby of the classy hotel where we decided to treat ourselves after our previous stop in Kanpur. 

Tomorrow I will see the Taj Mahal, the most iconic building in all of India, and from the cross-river views I saw of it today, I cannot wait to experience the fullness of its majesty up close.  I also find it fitting to get such a treat after coming so far over the last few days.  Not only is the physical distance impressive: Delhi to Varanasi on train and then Varanasi to Agra on motorcycle, but so too has been the distance in state of mind.  I am sure more trials stand before me here in India – in fact, this is one of the few things about which I am certain.  But it is also important to recognize and to celebrate challenges overcome, and so tonight and tomorrow morning I do celebrate.  And then, I get back on the road.

January 13, 2012
