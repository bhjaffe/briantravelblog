48 Hours in Cuenca

I kept waiting for something really story-worthy to happen to me to write my first impressions of Ecuador, the city of Cuenca, and the Spanish language program I'm doing here, but almost every example I could think of is something I didn't actually want to have happen to me.  For example, I didn't get stuck all day after my redeye from New York (as I had feared due to my original flight schedule) in a rundown airport with nothing to eat but questionable meat-on-a-stick sold by street vendors.  In reality, the agents at LAN Airlines were so friendly they practically escorted me through the domestic terminal in Quito, where I changed planes, and got me on a flight to Cuenca six hours earlier than my original ticket.  Similarly, I didn't wait anxiously after landing, watching every other passenger from my flight depart Cuenca airport and wondering if the message of my early arrival had been properly understood.  As it turned out, a quick Skype call on a conveniently unlocked wireless network combined with judicious use of an internet translator did the trick: my host brother picked me up promptly, speaking nearly perfect English, and within minutes I discovered that he studied as an exchange student three years ago in my home state! 

The pleasant discoveries have continued.  My host family lives in a very nice apartment fifteen minutes from my school and the old colonial part of the city.  All of them - mother, father, son of 21 years, daughters of 18 and 15, and even their tiny dog - are very friendly and welcoming (seriously, even the dog).  They've had a few students from the same program stay with them in the past, so they understand what my schedule will be like even better than I do.

The Spanish school has likewise exceeded my expectations so far.  For starters, it is located in what must be the nicest building in the entire colonial part of the city.  It's not very big, only occupying the bottom floor of the building, but there is a small garden overlooking the river where I had my first class (and got sunburned - I forgot how quickly the sun can do that at 8000+ feet of elevation), a small common area for students between classes, and several small classrooms.  The number of students also surprised me; there are only about a dozen students each in the morning and afternoon sessions to split between four or five teachers.  I am a morning student, which means I start class at 7:45 and finish around noon.

Apparently I did well on the entrance exam, because I've been placed in the intermediate class - that is, with the one other intermediate morning student who has already been here for a week.  She is from Switzerland and is very nice, although I think she must have learned much more last week by herself, because regardless of my exam results, I still struggle to say even the simplest sentences in Spanish and she’s forced to just sit there and wait as I stumble my way through everything I say.  We have one grammar class and one conversation class each day, each 90 minutes long, and following that I have 45 minutes of private instruction that I hope will get me past my inability to remember even the simplest vocabulary and verb conjugations.  I seriously question how I got labeled intermediate, but at least my comprehension seems to be getting better.

Curiously, almost everyone in the morning session is Swiss, and so last night I found myself out as the only non-Swiss in a large group that alternated between speaking Swiss-German, English (for my benefit) and our shared elementary Spanish.  We visited an old hat factory, toured the small tourist market in the city center, and ended the night with drinks and dinner at a little restaurant catering to the local expat and tourist crowd.  My Swiss companions are all very friendly, although I do wonder if they tire of my presence because otherwise they could just speak their native language together.

So in all, while nothing extraordinary has happened during my first two days here, I have high hopes for my stay in Cuenca.  The school plans evening and weekend cultural activities that I've signed up for, and one of the Swiss girls is already trying to plan a group trip to the coast next weekend.  I've also made inquiries into renting a motorcycle, as I am determined to explore the surrounding countryside after seeing the grass-covered mountainscape from the airplane as we approached for landing.  Meanwhile, my Spanish can really do nothing but improve, and I also have a cuddly dog who loves to sit next to me in my host family's armchair, so for now at least, those "I can't believe that happened!" stories can wait.

October 18, 2011
