I have seen the devil and I have walked on clouds, and all I had to do was go to Bolivia.

Part I: The Dark

The devil lives in the Cerro Rico Mines, high above the city of Potosi, Bolivia.  These mines are so infamous they were designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1987 for the grimmest possible reason: that over the last four-hundred fifty years more than eight million people have died in this mountain, and to this day more than nine thousand miners still enter its depths in search of the remnants of oar left behind by the generations who have gone before them.

While still dangerous and a quite undesirable place to earn a living, the mountain is now home to several dozen mining cooperatives, each in charge of its own system of tunnels.  This is in stark contrast to the Spanish colonial administration of the mines, which essentially enslaved the native population, forcing them to remain underground for months at a time, and then, when their numbers were insufficient, importing African slaves who were treated in the same manner.  And while their Spanish overloads once boasted Potosi as the richest city in the world, the workers in the mines almost certainly perished in extraordinary numbers in the exact same tunnels I was about to visit.  It was only with this understanding that I began to comprehend not only the dismal statistics about this place, but also the peculiar and seemingly self-destructive practices that still govern life within it. 

Our tour began at 9 am, after an all night bus ride to Potosi during which only a few of my group actually got a restful sleep.  Being therefore not only tired but also forewarned that the mines were not a particularly enjoyable place to visit, more than half of my group opted to skip the tour entirely.  The remaining six of us loaded into a small van to ride up to the mountainside.  Our tour leader was a local woman of perhaps 45 who stood less than five feet tall and had a rather abrupt personality despite her miniature stature.  This was far from her first day in the mines, a reality we only fully appreciated later in the tour.  Our first stop was actually still in town, where we put on blue plastic overclothes and rubber boots, and grabbed the hard hats and headlamps we would use once in the mines.  We then stopped at a mining outfitter where we got our first taste of miner culture.  We were buying gifts for the miners of the cooperative we were going to visit, and our choices were the following (in order of preference): coca leaves, pure alcohol (195 proof), hand rolled cigarettes, and juice.  Coca leaves were by far the most encouraged gift to bring, as it was explained to us that an individual miner would chew up to a grapefruit sized bag stuffed with coca leaves every day in the mine.  This would not only act as a stimulant to allow him to work upwards of 24 hour straight, but also suppress his appetite, as it was also explained to us that a miner would bring no food with him into the mine.  Not wanting to carry the heavy juice bottles, I purchased four packs of nine cigarettes each.  They cost me together less than one dollar.

Thirty minutes later we were on the mountainside, and my appreciation for just what mining does to both man and environment was only beginning to flesh out.  The mountain was a complete wasteland.  Having been reduced in height an incredible 300 meters (~1000 feet) over the centuries, none of its features looked natural.  A red-brown slope of gravel and broken rock was littered with plastic bags and other garbage as far as the eye could see.  There were tiny stone structures perched on the few patches of flat land where I later learned miners and even some of their families would spend the nights to guard the mineshafts from thieves.  The weather was drizzly, and pools of dark red and orange collected in the roadway.  Despite all this and the evident misgivings of the rest of the group, I was actually quite excited to enter the mine, my mood perhaps being a romantic throwback to childhood vacation prohibitions of exploring abandoned mineshafts that I came across in rural Idaho.  The romance, however, would not last much longer.

We entered the mine through an unceremonious archway in the mountainside walking single file between narrow trolley rails.  We were told this was one of the oldest mines on the mountain, dating back to the sixteenth century, but surprisingly the main entryway was still barely wider than my arm span.  As my eyes adjusted to the darkness and the dim light coming from my headlamp, I saw that the ceiling was a continuous stone archway like in a crypt of an old stone church, giving credence to its claimed age.  The ground was muddy, and in patches we walked through puddles of standing water.  We rounded a slight corner and the backlit glow of the entrance disappeared.  I was second in line behind the tour leader, and she marched along rapidly, having no need to bend over as the ceiling steadily diminished in height.

The air was still and cold in the first tunnels we entered, but as we continued deeper the faint smell of chemicals grew.  I tried not to dwell on it.  This was a tour for me; miners breathe this air every day for decades, so I was not going to complain.  Nevertheless, like the feeling of hair sticking up on the back of your neck, a slowly building sense of discomfort grew in me as each turn in the tunnel revealed another seemingly endless hole into the darkness.  The ceiling changed and was now bare exposed rock.  Our guide stopped and pointed her headlamp upward.  The ceiling twinkled with the reflection of mineral infused ore.

We walked farther, deeper into the mine, through long stretches where I had to crouch low to avoid hitting my head on the wooden support beams above me.  To the right or left even smaller shafts would branch off the main route into the darkness.  Sometimes makeshift wooden grates barred access, either to protect an individual miner’s worksite or to prevent passage into a tunnel no longer receiving outside air from the hoses running overhead.  Even in the main ventilated shaft, air quality was definitely worsening, and between that and the increasingly small passages, the line of fellow visitors behind me was getting anxious.  “How much farther?”  Became a frequent question.  “Just ten more minutes,” was always the answer from our guide.  “We’ve almost reached the Diablo.” 

I wrinkled my brow in bewilderment; had she just said we’ve almost reached the Devil?  I convinced myself I must have heard it wrong, but just then she said it again, this time using the English word.  I could feel the wave of anxiety behind me grow considerably.  What was she talking about?  Where was she taking us?  And then, just when we were all trying to convince ourselves that there must have been some sort of mistranslation to explain this strange word choice, we turned a corner a there he was: the devil in all his demonic glory.

At the back of an alcove dug about ten feet deep perpendicular to the main tunnel sat a life-sized wooden carved idol of the devil, complete with horns, red eyes, teeth in a ghoulish open-mouthed smile, arms, legs and an unavoidable foot-long erect penis.  What was creepier was that he was covered with a myriad of adornments, none of which looked old.  Colored streamers hung from his horns, half-smoked cigarettes sat in his mouth, fresh coca leaves covered his lap, and a dead llama fetus rested at his feat.  Two diagonal benches were carved out of the tunnel wall extending away from him, making this place a bona fide shrine to the devil.  Happy to have reached our destination, if only because it meant not walking farther into the mine, the group crowded onto the shrine benches and one by one had a double take of just what we were gathered before.  The sight was repulsive, but strangely nobody could break his or her captivated gaze.  As more headlamps focused on it, the details of the idol become even easier to see.  What made people put this here?  Was life in the mines really this sinister?  Then came the explanation.

Our guide began to recount how the Tio, the devil statue’s proper name, had come to the Cerro Rico Mines.  When the Spanish colonists first began mining the mountain they adopted the Inca tradition of conscripting local indigenous populations and forced them to work in the mines for three months out of every year.  Eventually, as the deadly conditions in the mines became widely recognized, the local inhabitants refused to work there, and the Spanish were forced to implement harsher methods of coercion.  First, they simply locked conscripted workers in the mines by controlling all the entrances.  Then, when the workers continued to rebel, they turned to the Church for help.  Having adopted Catholicism in conjunction with their traditional beliefs during the Spanish conquest, the local population was very receptive to the teachings of the local clergy, and out of this came the idea that what was needed in the mines was a deity – a god of the mines to reward the industrious and to punish the weak and lazy – and so the Spanish built idols of the devil and placed them in the mines.  The indigenous miners couldn’t pronounce the D in Dio – God in Spanish – so they gave their new idols the closest name they could pronounce: Tio, and they showered him with gifts to secure their safety and good fortune inside the mountain.

This story was compelling and logical enough, but it also took place hundreds of years ago.  Why was there still a Tio in the mine today?  It was probably just a tourist attraction, I thought.  Surely nobody actually believed in the power of a wooden statue today. 

I was wrong again. 

The next thing that happened is something I know I will never forget.  Starting as an explanation of sorts, our tour guide told us how miners would use coca leaves, alcohol and cigarettes to venerate the Tio.  But then – and in a form way too familiar and sincere to be just for show – she actually performed the ceremony.  First she took a handful of coca leaves and sprinkled them across the idol’s lap and around his feet.  Then she pulled out her tiny bottle of pure alcohol and poured a splash over each limb, ending with a splash on his penis “for fertility” as she had just explained.  Next she took a swig of the alcohol herself and made a pronounced smacking of her lips to show that she was strong enough to survive in the mines.  Finally, she pulled out a hand rolled cigarette, lit it, took a few puffs, and then placed it inside the Tio’s mouth, wedging it between two of his ugly wooden teeth.  All the while doing this she addressed the Tio saying, “This is for you Tio.  Make me strong and give me good luck.  Bring more tourists to the mines for me.” (She actually snickered as she said this, and then turned back to us with a quick, “because I’m not a miner” to make sure we understood the substitution in request.)  When she finished and sat back down looking at us she would have seen six dumbstruck expressions staring back at her if not for the blinding affect of so many headlamps fixated on her grinning face.  Then finally, as if to extinguish the last bit of hope that what we had just witnessed might have only been a demonstration for us tourists and not a real practice, the second tour guide, who to this point had been silently following at the back of the group, stepped forward and pulled out her own bag of coca leaves and bottle of a alcohol and quickly repeated the rite, this time in almost a whisper and completely in Spanish.  “Who wants to go farther?” asked the main guide obliviously.

It turned out most people had had enough.  Of the six of us, four took this opening to opt for the early exit from the mine.  We had walked for over half an hour straight into the mountain, but we still hadn’t reached a single miner.  Their worksites were still much deeper and also our remaining destination.  As the four others gave my one remaining companion, a smiley Australian girl, and me their gifts for the miners that we stuffed into our now overfilled jackets, we turned toward our guide and the tunnel extending beyond her.  “Ready?” she said.  “Ready or not, here we come,” was all I thought as I gave her a nod.

As I told the others later, it was a good decision to turn around if the tunnels before the Tio made you nervous, because it got way worse after.  Within minutes I was crawling through a passage no more than two feet high, and on the far side the air was decidedly staler.  Then it got hot.  Yes, whatever fire stokes the center of the earth was now also warming the tunnels I was crawling through.  I began to sweat.  We waded through ankle deep water that reflected in blood red when illuminated by my headlamp.  “Oxidation,” said our guide as we paused to look at the pool around our feet.  She shined her lamp on the trolley rail – it had coin-sized holes corroded into it.  “Bad for metal,” she said.  “And how about for people?”  I thought silently. 

Then in front of me I saw a flash of light and I inhaled rapidly with anticipation.  Please be the miners we’d been looking for!  It was them.  More lights appeared around the corner and a minute later we reached a chamber with half a dozen miners fixing the axle of an overturned metal trolley cart.  We exchanged greetings briefly and my companion and I quickly reached into our jackets to give them some of the gifts we’d brought, but in general the intrigue was all one-way.  I couldn’t help thinking that these miners may not appreciate the reminder that their work was so inhumane it had become a tourist attraction.  More miners would be nearby we were told, so we continued farther into the hot darkness.  Would these mines ever end?  Sweat now dripping down my face and feeling the wetness of a soaked tee shirt against my skin, I was reaching my limit.  How much farther were we going?  More tunnels, more red puddles, more stale air.  Above us I couldn’t help noticing that every third wooden support beam had cracked under the weight above it.  Finally we reached another group of miners hoisting a trolley full of oar from a tunnel descending into the darkness at a 45-degree angle.  Perhaps this was the planned end of the tour, or perhaps it was our eagerness to unload the remaining gifts that gave away our anxiety, but either way, after meeting this group our guide cheerfully announced that we were turning around and heading back to the entrance.  I had made it; I would be invited no further.  And with an insuppressible smile on my face I turned my back on the darkness in front of me and began retracing my steps into the darkness I had already come through.

With the anxiety of entering the unknown now washed away, the return journey was much easier.  With just about every turn the air felt a bit cooler and a bit cleaner.  Even the sections with extremely low ceilings were easier, knowing that I had already passed through them all once before.  We passed a few other tour groups still on their way into the mines.  I couldn’t help studying the expressions on each of their faces.  Had I looked so uneasy on my way in?  Sensing that we would soon be near the entrance, I halted at a patch of fallen rocks and made my one request of the tour.  Addressing both the guide and my Australian companion, I asked if they wouldn’t mind, just for a few seconds, if we all turned off our headlamps to experience the pitch black of the mine.  They agreed.  As the last headlamp clicked off the world around me went instantly dark.  It was the darkest dark I have ever experienced. 

What sort of world was this?  Why would people ever choose to be in such a place?  Was it the shockingly sincere devil worship I had witnessed earlier or the knowledge that the very tunnels I was transiting were built by the same enslaved people for whom they were made living tombs that made it the more sinister?  We turned our lights back on, and a few minutes later we exited the mine through that same unpronounced archway we had entered two hours before. 

I never want to go back inside that place, but my question is, does anyone?  Do these miners have any true desire to work these mines or is it simply the last choice before them?  It was raining when I exited, and even though I would normally hurry to my sheltered destination, I walked slowly and contently, feeling like I was already back in the shelter.  I was out of the mountain, and every raindrop that hit my sweaty face reminded me of this.  I took a deep breath of cool air.  I am out of the mines forever.  How long will it take for those behind me to feel the same as I do now?
