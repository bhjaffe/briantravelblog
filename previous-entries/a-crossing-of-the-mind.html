A Crossing of the Mind

« The Unexpected Gifts of Travel

The Perfect Destination »

Thoughts, plans, discussions, reflections, reassessments, and conclusions – I never imagined these would be my souvenirs of an overland crossing of the Australian Outback.  Yet over the last two weeks, as I covered five thousand kilometers of rural highway through one of the most sparsely populated regions on the planet not covered by ocean, ice or sand, my mind opened, as if put in a trance by the endless flat landscapes and straight roads upon them, and through this opening seemingly every thought, fear and aspiration for my future came rolling to the forefront of my consciousness, squawking for attention like little baby birds and vying to be first to jump out of the nest of my mind and into the air of my ultimate action.

The fact is, after traveling nonstop for five and a half months, I am finally approaching the end.  One month from today I will board an airplane in Hong Kong and return to the United States.  What happens after that – every next step from leaving that airplane onward – has yet to be determined.  A blank page awaits me, and it is as terrifying as it is exhilarating. 

Of course, it’s not as if I just woke up two weeks ago in Melbourne and realized for the first time that I would have a life after traveling, but up until then two powerful forces allowed me to keep decisions about the future more or less at bay.  First was the simple yet powerful force of distraction.  When I spent my days dodging traffic on crowded Indian highways, marveling over centuries-old palaces and tombs, or exploring lively modern cities my mind was full enough.  By contrast, the Australian Outback is an area so flat, huge and homogenous that from one distant horizon to another the most exciting change may be the presence of slightly more trees or a new species of grass upon the plane.  Second was the illusion of endless time.  Why worry about a decision that need not be made for months when there are so many more fun things to think about in the present?  Daydream or ponder?  Sure, I did plenty of that in regard to my future plans, but I never had to decide anything.  In fact, I couldn’t have even decided anything if I’d wanted to because too many variables such as which graduate schools would admit me still existed a few months ago.  Now all of that is rapidly changing, and the time to move from pondering to deciding is upon me.

Another unavoidable contributor to my newly focused contemplation has been sharing this great overland trek with two great friends facing similar question about their futures.  My friend Lane, with whom I shared the drive from Melbourne to Alice Springs, is quitting his job at the end of the year and knows only that he doesn’t want to stay in Hawaii where he currently lives.  From Alice Springs onward I’ve traveled with my best college friend, Kate, who is also weighing graduate school or changing jobs against staying at her current firm.  Sitting next to these two friends in the cab of our RV for ten hours or more, day after day, put every subject on the table, and nothing came up more frequently or more intensely than our respective hopes and concerns for the next stages of our lives. 

While obviously these sorts of conversations can and do happen anywhere, I also believe now, at the far end of my Outback crossing, that sometimes it takes a special combination of isolation and pleasant monotony to let the process of discernment take its due course.  Life can – and generally does – move too fast to stop and take stock of what you really want and how you really want to move toward it.  So even though I did more exciting things over the past fourteen days than stare ahead at the slowly changing scenery alongside a generally dead-straight road, it was that soaking up of the rugged, beautiful, open, unspoiled, almost dreamlike expanses, greater in size than even most countries of the world, that allowed me to take stock of my own dreams and aspirations and to face the future I have known for a long time awaits me at the end of this journey with a newfound confidence and resolve.  Did I need to come all the way to this remote part of the world to achieve this feeling?  Perhaps not, but it is nevertheless where I found it, and because of this I know the Australian Outback and my journey across it will hold a special place in my memory for a long time to come.

March 23, 2012
