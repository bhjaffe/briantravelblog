PumaNegra and My First Real Ride »

This past week was the mid-point of my five-week stay in Cuenca, and it was also a turning point of sorts for my social life here.  

The first great development was to make my motorcycle expeditions double as social expeditions.  Last Sunday I convinced Brandi, one of the American college students who studies at the same school as me but in the afternoon session, to take the leap of faith and ride with me on my motorcycle to El Cajas National Park.  We met early that day before there was much traffic, and within minutes of escaping the city we were climbing through the cold misty morning air of a long and narrow glacier-cut valley.  El Cajas is a collection of high mountain ridges and lake filled drainages perched atop the South American Continental Divide.  With the highway pass around 13,000 feet elevation, I was somewhat concerned about PumaNegra’s ability to carry us both up the steep ascent, but she mustered all 200 cc’s of engine capacity and kept us going with only a few sputters of inconsistency.  Granted, at forty kilometers per hour maximum speed we weren’t getting anywhere quickly, but the scenery was great and so was the sense of adventure.  Once we arrived at the trailhead on the far side of the pass we took a long hot chocolate break to warm up, and then went on a several hour hike from the lower elevations of the park to the summit of Tres Cruces next to the highway pass.  Then, with the help Brandi’s smile and a friendly semi truck driver, we made our way back to where the motorcycle was parked and enjoyed another incredibly scenic ride, this time back down the mountain to Cuenca.

The next day in class I had what I considered to be quite the story to share as we recounted our weekend activities, yet without really meaning to, I apparently gave off the impression to my classmate and teacher of being that reckless sort of foolhardy American tourist looking for a good time at any cost and without regard to risk or convention.  I was surprisingly angered by what I felt was unfair and prejudicial stereotyping on their part.  However, apparently not everyone who heard about my motorcycle outing was turned off to the idea, because later that same afternoon, and with much less convincing than had been required with Brandi, another student, this time a Swiss girl named Simone, joined me for yet another motorcycle adventure.

On this trip we retraced the back country roads of my first rural ride, and then continued over the mountain landscape until we got to a small colonial styled city in a valley about forty kilometers from Cuenca.  Like my first long ride, the landscape was that of panoramic views mixed with sharp turns, steep climbs and descents, lots of dust, dogs, cows and farmers.  The craziest part of this ride was actually the willingness of dogs to chase us down the bumpy unimproved roads.  More than once I had to speed away as fast as possible and still the chasing dog would be on our heels, and one downhill chase even went on so long that I nearly didn’t break in time for an upcoming hairpin turn.  The greatest part of this outing, however, was the drive back along the highway next to the river.  The journey out had taken us so long that by the time we started our return trip the sun was already going down.  As we drove through the narrow Santa Barbara River canyon all scenery became silhouetted as the cloud-filled sky beyond turned to an almost blinding flame of red and pink.

All the while daytime motorcycling became significantly more fun for me, another development made evening socializing much more fun as well.  This was the coming of the Fiestas de Cuenca.  Each year the first week in November is a citywide festival to celebrate the combination of All Saint’s Day on the 1st and Cuenca’s Independence Day on the 3rd.  People from all over Ecuador, and indeed from many parts of South America, converge on Cuenca for this week of celebration.  Free form markets and band stands are set up along any major street with a wide enough sidewalk, parades cross the city each morning, and fireworks shoot from various parks and squares each night.

With the streets literally packed with people by sundown, my fellow students and I were forced to break from our otherwise predictable pattern of going to the same expat restaurant and bar nearly every night, and we joined the swollen crowd of partiers in the city’s central district.  Also, the afternoon session American students, who rarely wanted to go out after their classes that finish at 6:15 pm, recognized that this was not the week to stay at home, so for a change I had some native English speakers to go out with.  I hadn’t realized just how much of a difference this would make!  Almost instantly I changed from the quiet and mild mannered self that had become my custom in the company of Team Swiss to a lively, talkative and, if I may so humbly suggest, way more fun person.  Having inadvertently, though not unhappily, separated from the Team Swiss on the night of the biggest parties, three other Americans, a newly arrived Swedish student and I walked all around the central square, explored new and fun bars, and even made friends with an indigenous Ecuadorian guy of our age who plays in a traditional Andean music band. 

It was during this encounter with the local musician, quite late and several drinks into the evening, that I had my first real experience of the kind I’d been hoping for ever since arriving in Ecuador.  This was the feeling of genuine communication across the boundaries of language and culture.  Who knows, maybe it was the alcohol or the mix of adrenaline still in my system from having just witnessed two fights break out in front of me, but for those fifteen minutes, when my new friends and I sipped on the local Cuencan aperitif and talked with this tattooed, dark skinned Ecuadorian with a chiseled figure and a long ponytail about music, growing up in Ecuador, being from an indigenous family, traveling in Europe with his band, and any other number of topics, I felt like we were all really communicating with each other and not just sharing information required for some sort of required transaction.  And when he started speaking his native language of Quechua, just to show us how it sounded, and then switched back into Spanish, I also had the great satisfaction of realizing I was having this experience in the language I only twenty days earlier had awoken from five years of hibernation. 

The night moved on, and before long I was in a cab headed back to my host family’s apartment, but the feeling I had captured stayed with me.  And even though the very next day my friends and I skipped the night’s fiestas to cook dinner and watch a British movie together in the comfort of my host family’s home, and the following morning we left for a weekend trip to a mountain hostel catering to westerners, I know that I have gotten something very special and unique out my time here in Cuenca – much, much more than I would have just passing through as a tourist - and I still have two more weeks to experience even more.

November 5, 2011
