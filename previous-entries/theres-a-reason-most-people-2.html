There's a Reason Most People Don't Do This

Roads (The Great Democratizer) »

I am sitting on a yellow wooden luggage cart at sunset in the cargo terminal of the New Delhi train station, and I am completely alone.  Before me in dusty muted colors move a series of interrelated streams of men and machines.  A deafening locomotive screams its whistle as it lumbers down the track toward the heart of the station to my left.  On another track a line of blue train cars slowly accelerates toward open railroad and its future destination to my right.  Overhead on an elevated walkway cross a constant march of pedestrians back and forth over the station’s dozen parallel tracks.  And over my left shoulder a line of luggage carts identical to the one I’m sitting on except for the enormous loads of packages and burlap covered goods piled high upon them stand temporarily static while their porters await the passing of the departing train to cross the tracks in front of me.  Yes, I am sitting here alone, without the motorcycle I rode here on, without the cell phone that has become my lifeline of information, and without my friends with whom I have been traveling, and yet despite all this, I can sit here contently with a newfound faith that all will work out if it is indeed meant to be so combined with an equally new resigned acceptance that nothing here in India can be compared with expectations from back home, because this is a different world, and here a different set of rules apply.

I wouldn’t always have been able to stomach my present situation so calmly.  Indeed, even three days ago the vulnerability and exposure of my present predicament, being, despite my best effort at conspicuousness, the clear favorite for beggars, onlookers, and even stray dogs, would have severely unnerved me, but a lot has happened in the last seventy-two hours to change my perspective, and even though it has been a trial of extreme frustration and many defeats, I think I am finally beginning to turn a corner on what it means to be traveling in the real India and what sort of attitude is required to succeed as a traveler here.

The reason I say the real India is because not everyone would choose to travel the way I am traveling.  Indeed, most westerners would never have even attempted it.  However, many months ago my best friend Nick and I decided, albeit with a heavy dose of naïve ignorance, that the capstone of our pre-graduate school travel would be to tour India on Royal Enfield motorcycles, and now, all those months later, here we are about to take a one-way train ride hundreds of miles from our nearest contact into the very heart of India with nothing more than a couple bags, two rented motorcycles, and Nick’s friend Sally as a companion for our first ten days of traveling.  As desired, adventure is surely before us, but even getting to this point already seems like a small miracle when looking back on it. 

Three days ago I was in the city of Patiala in the northern Indian state of Punjab attending a wedding reception for my sister and her newly wedded Indian husband.  The reception was at an old palace that is now a hotel, and needless to say every available form of comfort and ease was being afforded to us.  Delicious breakfasts met us in the mornings, drivers chauffeured us around town as necessary, and a seemingly endless supply of tea and drinks were constantly being offered to us.  Life was good and life was simple, that is, until we set out for our return journey to Delhi the following day.

Our trip from Delhi to Patiala had been our maiden motorcycle voyage in India, and the experience proved much more difficult than expected; however, with tips from my brother-in-law’s family and lessons learned from our first outing, we were confident that the return trip to Delhi would go much easier.  This was true so much as that the transit from Patiala to the outskirts of Delhi that had been so trying in the opposite direction two days before flew by with relative ease.  However, once we reached Delhi the indisputable complication of navigating a completely foreign metropolis took over, and our apparent good fortune made a distinct turn for the worse. 

We arrived in Delhi around three thirty in the afternoon and our first task was to swing by the motorcycle shop from where we’d rented the bikes to switch out the silver motorcycle I’d been riding for a red, slightly nicer one we’d arranged to rent a month prior but that had been unexpected unavailable the week before.  Swapping one motorcycle for another: how long should this take?  The answer: two and a half hours minimum.  To his credit, the owner of the motorcycle shop, Lalli Singh, is an invaluably honest and reliable businessman without whom I am certain this entire expedition would not be possible.  However, speed is not his forte, and as the sun set somewhere outside his basement shop floor we were still sitting in small wooden chairs in front of his desk wondering what was possibly taking so long.  Finally completing the swap we rode to our next errand destination, the hotel I had been staying at prior to my trip to Patiala.  My purpose there was to retrieve items I’d left behind in my room safe, and once again, all expectations were that this would be a very quick in-and-out affair.  Wrong again.  For some reason this quick retrieval took over forty minutes as one reception attendant after another assured me that my stuff was “on the way” as I waited dumbly in the lobby for something to actually happen.  On a normal day this would have been only a minor inconvenience, but to Nick, Sally and I, who had been on the road since ten in the morning and had not eaten a proper meal since nine, hunger, frustration and anxiety were building steadily, and our final task of the evening: finding the hotel we’d booked in central Delhi, still stood before us.  To add to it all a drizzle that had been threatening all day was starting to turn to a steady rain.

This is when things really got bad.  We made our way back in to the crowded narrow streets of one of the bazar districts in central Delhi where Google Maps showed our hotel being located.  At this point we knew better than to expect street signs, but we’d planned ahead and drawn some detailed maps, so we at least had confidence that we could get within asking distance of the hotel’s location.  This turned to be a total nightmare.  First off, Nick and I, frustrated and tired, had different ideas of how to get there, and we also both thought the other was understanding of who would be leading the way.  This led us to get temporarily separated in the midst of the choking Delhi traffic, and when we met up we both had heated tempers and harsh words for whose fault it was we had almost lost each other.  A few minutes later we entered the bazar and our entire definition of dense traffic was redefined as the mixture of people, rickshaws, horse drawn carriages, and motorcycles trying to squeeze down incredibly narrow streets made going faster than a crawl absolutely impossible.  To add to this my new bike wouldn’t idle properly, so the slow speeds led to it stalling almost every other minute, leaving me temporarily stranded behind Nick and Sally who were riding in front.  Sure enough, one stall too many coupled with being stuck behind the world’s slowest ox cart separated me from Nick and Sally, and given that the sheer density of street traffic kept line of sight to something around twenty-five feet, no sooner had I looked up than I realized their black motorcycle was nowhere in sight.  I tried frantically to make up ground through the gridlock of men and beasts, but it was to no avail.  They were gone!  I was alone in a Delhi bazar with nothing but a cheap tourist map and an address of a hotel.  I asked for directions from anyone who looked like they might know English, but they all told me I was miles away from the hotel.  Should I trust them and go back by the direction I’d come from, or should I trust my own sense of direction having studied the hotel’s location on Google Maps?  What about Nick and Sally?  Had this really become every man for himself?  Just as my panic began to mount I heard my name being shouted from over my shoulder.  It was Nick.  He and Sally had emerged from somewhere, equally unsuccessful in finding the hotel. 

After one more dead end we decided to backtrack at the insistence of a rickshaw driver, and before we knew it we were out of the bazar altogether crossing over the railroad yard into a completely different neighborhood.  At least this one looked promising, because every other building had bright neon lights with hotel names reflecting off the now wet roadway.  But where was ours?  We stopped and asked for more directions, and more directions, and people tried to take us to their own hotels instead until said we insisted we already had reservations.  Then I dropped my bike on the now incredibly slick street surface, screaming ahead to Nick as I went down so that he wouldn’t leave me behind again.  We went down smaller and shadier looking streets, passing by literally dozens of hotels, but none of them were ours, and once again, for the second time in three days, we were all at our wits end unable to finish another exhausting day of motorcycling.

Finally, out of the corner of my eye I caught a sign with the word Metropolis (our hotel’s name) on it, and we regained a glimmer of hope.  It turned out our actual hotel was still a block and a half away, but within ten minutes we were there and off the bikes we once again swore we’d never get back on no matter what the circumstances or how much someone were to pay us. 

After checking into our rooms we ate dinner and had a few beers at a nearby restaurant, but even though we thought our moods had improved, below the surface Nick and I were still both extremely frazzled and agitated by the day’s events.  This below-the-surface anger finally showed its ugly head just before we went to bed when he and I got into it with each other over the stupidest thing in a way that in all the years we’ve known each other has never once even come close to happening.  I went to bed furious and frustrated, angry and alone, and wondering why I had ever decided to undertake this trip that so many people had told me was a crazy idea all the previous months I’d been flaunting it as such a great adventure. 

The next day I woke up still angry, but after eating breakfast by myself I knocked on Nick and Sally’s room door, and as a testament to why Nick and I are such good friends, we sat down in my room (Sally was still asleep) and asked out loud, "What the hell is going on?"  The truth is, Nick and I, who to our friends and co-workers have both always been known as cool under pressure and level headed in the face of adversity, were both already being pushed to our limits by our motorcycle trip in India, and we had only just completed our self-titled shakedown ride.  How were we ever going to survive nearly two more months on the road?  Our biggest conclusion was that, like it or not, we had to reset our expectations and accept that lessons have to be learned, often the hard way, and that that’s part of what makes an adventure an adventure in the first place.  After all, we finally laughed, there’s a reason most people don’t do this. 

Over the next two days, from that conversation until right now, I have made efforts to both enjoy India for a change and to take precautions to avoid a repeat of what we’d just been through on our way to and from Patiala.  First I spent some time doing what most people center their entire Indian vacations around: seeing some of the truly amazing monuments around the city.  I also spent half a day tracking down and purchasing a GPS unit (which, even given the amount of time I invested in the project, still seemed like a total coup to have so expeditiously procured given the pace at which any transaction is accomplished here in India).  Finally, I called a guesthouse in Varanasi that had been highly recommended to me and arranged to have a driver waiting for us at the train station so we won’t have to try to navigate the horde of extortion artists and otherwise semi-informed, semi-English speaking passers by who await western tourists at every major sightseeing destination after arriving on the overnight train.   

A few hours ago a man from Lalli Singh’s motorcycle shop came and met us at our hotel and escorted us to the freight terminal from where the bikes are now being loaded onto the train.  Nick left half an hour ago to meet Sally, who was doing some last minute shopping around town, and so I’m here watching over our bags until they come back.  As I look over the ever moving mass of people all about me in this most unique of settings I do have a sense of pride that I’ve made it this far, that I’ve been able to succeed at some tasks over the last two days, and that despite the early trials of this journey I’m still eager to forge ahead with what comes next.  Although the word risks being overused, true adventure isn’t easy by definition, and I’ve come to appreciate this truth more in the last few days than in all of my travels yet to date.  I’m sure more hard time will come mixed with the good, more frustration will come mixed with the joy, and more anxiety will come mixed with the elation, but as long as that second half keeps coming I must insist sitting here, taking it all in, that this experience is still worth all the anguish and then some.

January 8, 2012
