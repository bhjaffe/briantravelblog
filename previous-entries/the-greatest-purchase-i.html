The Greatest Purchase I Have Ever Made

« An Adventure's End
Previous Entries
Dear India »

Traveling is a funny thing.  People who are normally accustomed to an established level of comfort and convenience provided by access to their various possessions decide to leave almost all of that behind and go to another location where they have to either rent access on a temporary basis to replacement items (bed, car, kitchen, etc.) or go without a degree of comfort and convenience (air conditioning, internet, etc.) for a duration of their travel.  Life is therefore inevitably more difficult while traveling than at one’s home.  To mitigate this barrow-or-go-without burden, however, travelers universally do another otherwise peculiar thing: they select a sub-set of their most prized and mobile possessions, those that they’re least willing to go without while away from home, and stuff them into backpacks, suitcases, handbags, duffel bags, and even clothing pockets in order to bring with them on their journeys.  These items are the not-live-withouts, the select few in lifestyles filled with possessions, and much thought goes into what makes the list and just how much burden it is worth to have access to all these things at all times. 

I’ve packed twice so far for my travels, once for South America before Christmas and again for India, Australia and China after Christmas, and both times I strove to only bring that which I would really need.  Yet no matter how sparing I attempted to be in my selection process, I inevitably packed some items that simply sit in the bottom of my duffel bag at each stop along the way and rarely if ever get used.  The thing is, packing is a form of guesswork: you make assumptions about what a place you’ve never been to will be like, and from those assumptions you decide what you’ll want most and what will be most difficult to purchase or barrow.  One wildly inaccurate assumption I made was how difficult laundry service would be.  I imagined that I would be washing underwear and shirts in hotel bathroom sinks along the way, so I purchased clothing made of quick drying fabric instead of regular cotton.  Once I started traveling, though, I realized any touristy town has a laundry services available, and getting clothes washed is usually as simple as dropping them off one day and paying a modest sum of money when you pick them up the next.  Another false assumption I made was that the Andes would be really cold, so I brought a sweatshirt, a thin down jacket, a leather jacket and a raincoat with me, half of which I could have left at home.

Other assumptions, however, led to excellent packing decisions.  For instance, I knew I would want a full sized computer on which to surf the internet, write my blog, and edit photographs and videos.  I also knew that I didn’t want to lug around anything very heavy, so I purchased a top of the line MacBook Air (the single most expensive thing I have with me by far), and it has been amazingly convenient and useful.  I also assumed that I wouldn’t be able to buy a comfortable motorcycle helmet (I have a ridiculously large head) or a specialized armor-padded riding jacket or gloves in Ecuador or India, so at a great expense of space in my duffel bag I purchased those items stateside and brought them with me.  This turned out to be a great call not only for the sake of comfort, but also because both times I have fallen off my motorcycle (once going up a steep muddy road in Ecuador, and the other when a previously parked rickshaw abruptly pulled out and blocked my lane in India) my protective gear spared me any physical harm and allowed me to simply shake off the incident and continue on my way. 

Other prized items have been purchased along the way.  After getting a sunburn during a forty-five minute outdoor Spanish lesson during my first week in Cuenca, Ecuador, I realized the combination of high altitude, equatorial sunshine that cuts through the atmosphere at a right angle and my pale skin were not going to bode well over the next two months, so I bought a Panama hat at the local hat factory and wore it every sunny day until I left it on a bus in Salta, Argentina two days before leaving South America.  Similarly, when I found myself chilly in the tiny town of Pushkar in Northern India I purchased a thin maroon and orange striped blanket to wrap myself in.  While I always expected to discard it once I left the cold desert nights of Rajasthan, I have kept “Pushkar blacket,” as it is now affectionately called, and I still use it frequently on cold nights in hotels that often give me nothing more than a top sheet on my bed. 

Items people bring with them while traveling are chosen according to their assumed value, and they are then used according to their actual value.  Those items with a lot of the former and little of the latter are the ones that find their ways to the bottom of our backpacks and suitcases and may undertake an entire journey unused.  Items of the opposite sense are almost never brought and are then either dearly missed or purchased along the way.  But the underlying question remains: what is value really?  Value is almost certainly not the purchase price of an item once it is bought, because almost nothing we own or bring with us will ever be resold.  For instance, I spent hours trying to recover my Panama hat after I left it in Salta, even though shipping it to Buenos Aires would have easily exceeded the twenty-five dollars I paid for it.  At one point in Varanasi, India I purchased seven zip-ties for fifteen rupees (thirty cents) each even though I knew I was being ripped off because I figured they would come in handy somewhere down the road.  (Sure enough, they are currently holding half of my horn to the front of my motorcycle after its metal mount broke in half.)  On the other hand, if you stole the fifty dollar pair of dark pants I’ve been carrying around unworn for the last two months I might not even notice for several days and certainly wouldn’t be very upset.  But then again, just because something isn’t used frequently doesn’t mean it isn’t important.  I carried a pair of lightweight Patagonia rain pants with me through all of South America and only used them once – but that one time was when I climbed Cotopaxi, a 19,000+ foot (5,800+ meter) volcano, in the middle of a snowstorm, and wearing those pants relieved me of the need to wear rental snow pants that were easily five times heavier and way less comfortable.  Value is therefore what something allows you to do.  The more it allows or the more difficult doing something would be without it, the more true value an item has. 

So what is the most valuable item I have with me?  That answer to that is clear beyond any shadow of a doubt: MayMyInida.  MapMyIndia is essentially just a Garmin GPS navigator for India, but it has made more possible over the last two months than I ever would have imagined before starting this journey.  When I first dreamt up the idea of motorcycling through India it was meant to be a grand adventure: something truly unique among all my life experiences so far, something challenging, something rewarding, and something I would never forget.  I was confident I was far from the first person to undertake such a journey, and a quick search of Google and YouTube confirmed this suspicion.  Plenty of people have motorcycled India, so surely it couldn’t be that difficult.  I even read a book about a fifty-year-old Indian man riding north to the world’s highest road while I was traveling through South America.  I am half his age, and I wouldn’t be traveling to any remote mountain passes either.  In other words, this journey was going to be completely doable.

That perspective changed dramatically after Nick’s and my first outing to Patiala and back for my sister’s wedding reception.  Those two days of riding were extremely unpleasant, in large part because on both occasions we got horribly lost and then even more confused when well intentioned but nevertheless equally clueless Indians tried to give us directions.  Things that would have been laughably easy in the United States such as finding a major highway or locating an address in a major city were infuriatingly difficult for someone who didn’t speak Hindi, and between that and the no-rules traffic of Indian roads, Nick and I seriously considered giving up on the entire motorcycle tour before it ever really began.  Instead we decided to continue, at least on the train to Varanasi, on the condition that we purchased a GPS, as had been recommended by my sister’s new brother-in-law at the wedding reception.  Sure enough, MapMyIndia changed everything.

At the beginning Nick and I kept our motorcycles on major highways, so we really only needed directions at the beginning of each day to find the highway leaving a particular city and then again at the end of the day to find whatever hotel we had picked from a guidebook or MapMyIndia’s built-in database.  But even that was huge.  Gone was the mystery of weaving through tiny, overcrowded roads hoping the directions given to us were accurate or that we were still on the right path.  Gone was the need to stop, inevitably attract a crowd of at least eight people, ask them how to get someplace, wait for them to discuss it among themselves, and then discern the consensus or most credible recommendation from their broken English.  This was even more important in the evenings when a sore body from a long day’s ride and anxiety over a soon-to-be-setting sun made tempers short and the need to be off the motorcycles as great as ever for everyone’s health and sanity.  MayMyIndia didn’t change the roads or reduce the dangers we dealt with daily upon them, but it did something almost as great: it removed the uncertainty, and that made a previously unbearable experience manageable. 

Over the last two weeks MapMyIndia has been even more crucial.  This is because here in Southern India it has made possible entire journeys we never would have attempted without it.  Ever since Nick and I met up with our friend Mike in Goa, our motorcycle touring has taken on a decidedly different pace and style.  Whereas the first month of our tour was dictated by an ever-present need to reach a certain city by a certain date, leaving Goa we had three weeks to travel a comparatively short distance to the city of Trivandrum, our ending point at the southern tip of India.  Along with this, the obvious tourist trail that Nick and I had been following from Varanasi through Agra, Rajasthan, and Mumbai more or less ends in Goa.  There was no obvious next destination aside from Karela, the state of which Trivandrum is the capital, and so with time to simply explore Southern India, we made plans the old fashioned way: the day before we wanted to leave a place we looked up surrounding cities in our secondhand Lonely Planet guidebook, found something that looked interesting, and then simply plugged the destination into MapMyIndia the next morning. 

Has MapMyIndia ever met the challenge too!  Whereas three quarters of our traveling prior to Goa had been on four-lane highways, leaving Goa we didn’t see anything wider than two lanes until yesterday when we reached the port city of Kochi in central Kerala, over a thousand kilometers of riding later.  MapMyIndia has taken us over deserted forest passes, through mile after mile of rice fields on narrow country lanes, and down one lane roads that reminded me more of fire lanes in a forested park than the state highways they purported to be.  Leaving the coast to visit the ancient imperial capital of Hampi we traveled through tiny villages and small towns and watched coastal forest turn to inland desert one unmarked intersection at a time.

While we often questioned whether MapMyIndia had decided to turn us off a nicely paved highway onto a poorly maintained road in order to shave off just a few kilometers, we have never actually been let down by its navigation.  We have always reached our destination, and the confidence gained therein has let us repeat our blind obedience and day-by-day planning. 

The culmination of this very off the beaten path travel was witnessing the annual Kampala, or water buffalo races, outside the city of Mangalore in the southwestern corner of the state of Karnataka.  Mangalore, though a reasonably sized port city, wasn’t even recognized as a destination when we tried to explain it the day before we arrived to hotel workers who were convinced we were talking about Bangalore instead.  Nevertheless, our butchered pronunciation didn’t keep MapMyIndia from knowing where we wanted to go, and so just as we had planned for over two weeks, the day before Kampala was set to begin, we arrived in Mangalore after yet another day of crisscrossing local Indian roads and highways.   The next day, this time with the help of a local taxi driver, we made our way to the Fajir Bus Stand – literally a bus stand thirty kilometers outside Mangalore where we had read online that Kampala was being held.  With our driver asking final directions from passersby and a building excitement within the cab that we might actually achieve the seemingly unachievable, our way continued from a two lane paved road to a one lane paved road to a one lane dirt road to… amazingly, in the complete middle of nowhere, an open field filled with parallel one hundred twenty-meter-long mud tracks, dozens of harnessed buffalo teams, and five thousand local spectators.

Kampala was by far the most unique thing I’ve witnessed in India, and easily one of the most unique things I’ve witnessed during all of my travels so far.  For the six hours we were there, and through the night and into the morning thereafter, teams of two male water buffalos, singularly for a clocked time or side by side for head-to-head matchups, sprinted down a track of eight-inch-deep water with a man holding a tether in one hand and wielding a bamboo cane in the other dashing behind them, yelling, beating, and trying not to be pulled over.  Along the raised sidewalls of the track, spectators crowded behind a rope barrier, vying for the best view of the action.  Nick, Mike and I were also the only non-Indians there.  Our conspicuousness, however, played to our unintended advantage when the announcer standing under a grandstand for VIPs suddenly switched into English and started saying, “My friends… my friends…” while gesturing toward us.  Before we knew it, we were offered front row seats in under a colored tarp surrounded by the local elders and business owners who financed the competition.  In that way the uniqueness of the spectacle before us was matched only by the warm reception and genuine hospitality of the event’s hosts, for whom the presence of three awe-struck Americans was an obvious source of pride in what they countless times described as their “own traditional culture” with happy smiles on their faces. 

The entire day we were at Kampala, MapMyIndia sat charging in our hotel room back in Mangalore.  Nevertheless, I know beyond any doubt that without MapMyIndia we would never have made it across hundreds of miles of Indian back roads on motorcycles to be in just the right place on just the right day to witness such an event.  MapMyIndia, though just a simple GPS console, has opened up the rest of India to us.  It has allowed us to pull back the curtain and see the real India, the India behind that witnessed at major tourist destinations. 

When I was in Goa I met an Italian woman about my age on a riverboat ride at one of the most touristy places I have been in India.  She explained to me that she had spent a few days in Mumbai and a week in Goa, and that after those two places she felt like she’d really gotten a feel for India and that she was checking it off her list of places to travel.  I tried not to laugh.  I certainly don’t want to tell anyone else how to see a new country, especially one as huge and diverse and challenging as India.  But to think that you have seen India – to think that you even begin to know or understand India after skipping from one tourist destination to anther is a laughable notion.  Over the last seven weeks I would say I’ve gotten an excellent sampling of what India is.  And over the last two weeks, in large part because of the confidence I’ve had to strike out into the heartland of Southern India, I would say I have come to really appreciate the diversity and culture of this region.  That is what MapMyIndia has done for me.  My computer keeps me connected, my motorcycle jacket keeps me safe, my Panama hat kept my face shaded, and my rain pants kept me dry and mobile.  But MapMyIndia gave me certainty.  It gave confidence to do what I never would have done otherwise.  It gave me a way to turn my motorcycle tour into the sort of adventure I had always hoped it would be.  And in that way it has given me more than any other singular purchase I have ever made. 

The tops of Rickshaw windshields in India are often adorned with prayers or names of deities or mantras for good luck and safety on the road.  These words are often written in scripts I can’t understand, but recently in Mangalore Nick and I noticed that “Infant Jesus” was a popular choice.  It got us thinking though, what would we put on the front of a rickshaw if we were driving one?  What would we worship or advertise or ask for good fortune?  In a land of a thousand forms of religious expression, in cities with temples, mosques, shrines and churches around the corner from each other, where would our allegiance lie?  Nick laughed that he would put “Little Baby Jesus” on his.  I said I’d put MapMyIndia, and he stopped laughing and agreed.  The Super Bowl may be brought to you by Doritos, and Sesame Street may be brought to you by the number 3, but India Motorcycle Tour 2012 is brought to us by MapMyIndia, and it seems only right to give it due praise.
