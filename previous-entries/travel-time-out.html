Travel Time Out

« The Trail Less Traveled

Cotopaxi »

Travel Time Out (noun) an imposed temporary suspension of travel activities, specifically the separation of an overcommitted, fatigued or otherwise disabled traveler from one or more other travelers as a method of recovery

I am in travel time out, and while the exact circumstances that put me here were beyond my control, I probably should have seen it coming from a ways away. 

It all started when I arrived in Lima.  I had just finished six wonderful weeks in Ecuador, and I was a bit nostalgic to be leaving both my friends and my sense of comfort within Ecuadorian society.  Earlier that evening, on the ride to the Quito airport, my taxi driver had even sung me a traditional Spanish folk song after seeing my guitar and chatting a bit about music.  I gave him a 25% tip and thanked him for the pleasant ride. 

In Lima everything felt different, and foreign.  What was worse, I had been suddenly demoted from a culturally aware gringo to a generic tourist, and my first task was to wait in line for over an hour to clear immigration.  It was late at night, I was tired, and I couldn’t believe how slowly things were moving.  I knew the next morning I would be back at this very airport to fly to Cusco, but my tour company had for some reason chosen a hotel forty-five minutes away as our rendezvous point.  After edging in front of a woman who was holding up an entire line of people while sifting through her purse for a customs form (and then refusing to acknowledge her as she spent the next five minutes trying to engage me in very angry Spanish), I finally made it out of the airport just before midnight.  An unlicensed cab driver immediately walked up to me and grabbed my bag, showing me to his waiting car.  I was too worn down to wait in another line so I went with him.  “How much?” I asked showing him the hotel address.  “Fifty dollars,” he said.  “Thirty or give me my bag back.”  Seeing that I wasn’t messing around and without saying another word he got in the car and we set off.  He got no tip either.

Travel Fatigue (noun) lessening in one's response to or enthusiasm for travel, typically as a result of overexposure to it

The next day I woke early and after a solitary breakfast I waited in the hotel lobby to meet the group I would be traveling with for the next four weeks.  This again, I thought to myself.  I longed to be back in the common room of my Spanish school in Cuenca, which, as the place where previous first encounters had taken place, had such a friendlier feel.  I had missed the official meet and greet the night before, and, from whom I gathered to be in my group of the many people mulling around the lobby, everyone seemed to be traveling as a couple.  Was I going to be the only solo traveler?  Why wasn’t I more excited?

By that afternoon I was in Cusco, the ancient capital of the Inca Empire, and by then the signs of travel fatigue were indisputable.  Had Cusco always been so damn touristy?  I had been here ten years ago after graduating high school, but now it felt different.  Seriously, I couldn’t walk more than five feet or look at an item for sale for more than five seconds as I walked around town without being aggressively pursued by a street vendor dressed in brightly colored traditional Andean clothing, no doubt also to entice me to make a purchase.  As I showed my new travel companions how the Spanish colonial buildings had been erected directly on top of the large, precision-cut Incan masonry three girls carrying baby alpacas practically ambushed us in hopes that we would take pictures with them for a healthy tip.

That night for dinner we went to a very fancy restaurant, and although my food was excellent, an inexplicable sharp pain in my upper right chest kept me from enjoying myself very much.  At least I was sitting at the end of the long table next to the wall so not everyone would notice me being agitated and antisocial. 

Despite spending the next day on a scenic and peaceful walk from one Inca ruin to another in the hilly countryside above the city of Cusco, that night I once again struggled to sleep as the frequency and intensity of my chest pain gradually increased.  Was this some sort of freak heart condition or just one of the myriad symptoms of mild altitude sickness?  WebMD assured me I at least wasn’t having a heart attack, and Advil seemed to lessen the pain.  Other people in the group were talking about getting poor sleep too, but chest pain?  This was getting strange.

Bonk (verb) reach a point of exhaustion or physical limitation that makes one unable to go further

The third day we were to leave Cusco by bus for the Sacred Valley of the Incas and begin the first step of hiking the Inca Trail to Machu Picchu (that hike being the main reason I came back to Cusco in the first place).  Yet unlike previous mornings, my chest pain hadn’t gone away after sleeping.  In fact, it had gotten worse, and it was now affecting my mood as much as my physical comfort.  I wasn’t having fun.  I was surrounded by happy couples and cheery Australian university students, and all I could think about was how I was alone with nobody to share any of this with.  As the bus climbed up the winding roads leaving Cusco I turned up my iPod to drown out the tour guide.  The bright morning sunshine that flashed between rooftops lit the bus window and made it reflective to the point that I could see my own face inches in front of me.  Who would want to talk to that unhappy person hidden beneath a goofy looking straw hat and dark aviator sunglasses anyway?  Not me.

I was in real pain, but I was trying to ignore it because I really wanted to do that hike.  If I could just get through the day maybe I’d feel better.  Besides, we would be staying at significantly lower altitude that night, so maybe my symptoms would go away.  By our second stop, however, this time to a hill-perched Incan settlement surrounded by those iconic terraces that stretched all the way down to the valley floor a thousand or more feet below, the denial game was up.  I couldn’t even walk around for twenty minutes without feeling like my entire right lung was on fire.  I had bonked and I needed help.  I spoke with my trip leader and she arranged a doctor from Cusco to meet me at the hotel that afternoon.

The doctor arrived at my hotel room around 4pm with a black leather bag in one hand and a motorcycle helmet in another.  He definitely had style for a house call figured out.  He spoke nearly perfect English, which was a relief, as previously I had been trying to remember all my anatomical vocabulary in Spanish.  In less than ten minutes of questioning and listening to my breathing he said in a very matter of fact way that I needed to return to Cusco to get a chest x-ray.  And just like that my Inca Trail trek vanished.  I was resigned to it.  I was in no shape to hike and I knew it.  Still, as I found myself in a cab half an hour later climbing the steep valley walls back toward civilization, I couldn’t help feeling a bit defeated.

The entire hospital experience, aside from the horrendous episode of Peruvian soap opera that I watched in a waiting room, was actually extremely pleasant and efficient, especially for it being almost 8pm by the time the motorcycle doctor and I got there.  We practically flew through a blood sample, three x-rays, and a consultation with a lung specialist.  The conclusion, after much discussion between the two doctors, was that I had a lung infection, and that the high altitude had not caused my symptoms.  However, just to be safe, the doctors wanted me to stay in Cusco for at least another day in case my condition deteriorated. 

Travel Time Out (noun) an imposed temporary suspension of travel activities, specifically the separation of an overcommitted, fatigued or otherwise disabled traveler from one or more other travelers as a method of recovery

Thus, almost in fulfillment of the pain-induced self-pity I’d had earlier in the day, I was now actually alone.  Alone in a Cusco hotel room, sick and in pain with a plastic bag full of prescription antibiotics and the telephone number of the doctor who said he’d stop by around noon the next day.  And yet, despite all this, my mood was much better than it had been in days.  Yes, I was in travel time out, but I probably needed it.  Both my mood and my health had been steadily deteriorating, almost certainly in concert with each other, ever since I’d arrived in Peru, and I needed to step away from travel for a bit to get them both back to where they needed to be.  I spent the next day lying in bed, finishing up a graduate school application, chatting with my parents and best friend over Skype, listening to some of my favorite music, catching up on internet news, and, as if a sign that fate too approved of my time out, happily reading two important email responses for which I’d been waiting frustratingly for several days. 

I am now two days into my recovery, and with the blessings of my doctor, I was allowed to rejoin my trip leader in the Sacred Valley of the Incas and to proceed, albeit by train, toward Machu Picchu tomorrow.  My chest still hurts a bit when I take heavy breaths, but the combination of rest and antibiotics definitely feels like it’s having the desired effect.  More importantly, however, I’m excited to be traveling again, and that may just have been worth this entire experience.

December 1, 2011
