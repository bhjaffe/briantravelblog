Real and Fake

« New and Old
Previous Entries
The Unexpected Gifts of Travel »

Made in China.  I have read these three words on the labels of clothing, electronics, cookware, toys, musical instruments – just about everything – for as long as I can remember.  In fact, I grew up knowing two facts about China long before any others: it is the most populous country in the world, and it is where everything is made.  Now that I am here, in the land that produced so many of the things I’ve enjoyed over the past twenty-eight years, I am perplexed by what to make of it all, because China appears to be spending its new treasure in an attempt to become more like the places it sends its exports and less like the proud and ancient country I have always imagined it to be.  Perhaps I expected China to be something of a mix of the two Asian countries I know best: India with its untamed vivacity laced through a complexly layered society, and Japan with its uniquely thoughtful modernity enjoyed by an industrious people.  China is neither, but I still haven’t figured out what it is instead.

One indisputable truth about China, though, is that it has forever been the creator of thing the world wants.  On the taxi ride from my arrival at the Shanghai Airport to my hotel in the city center I crossed a beautiful modern arched bridge over the Huangpu River with large Chinese characters written across the top of it.  I asked my friend Kate, who studied Chinese in college, what it meant and she responded, “Welcome to Shanghai… I think,” but I was already thinking of another bridge and chuckling to myself.  A few miles from our college campus in New Jersey I remembered there being an old rusty railroad bridge over the Delaware River with huge letters on the side saying, “Trenton Makes, The World Takes.”  That slogan would be much more appropriate here.

I didn’t come to China to be a consumer, but oddly, that quickly became the most fun thing to do in Shanghai.  After spending much of our first day wandering around what seemed to be more or less just another modern city with huge skyscrapers, I transformed into a sort of kid-in-a-candy-store when we entered the old town district.  Narrow streets bustled with the first serious crowds I’d encountered since arriving, and the decorative red wood building fronts, hanging lanterns and tile roofs were unmistakably Chinese.  Small shops selling all sorts of traditional items – many for tourists but others clearly intended for local shoppers – occupied hundreds of narrow storefronts.  Before long I was haggling over the price of small wooden boxes, decorative fans, and small tapestries and paintings.  I joked to my friends, “This was the best China Town I’ve ever seen!”

Over the next two days my friends and I visited several other tourist destinations and commercial districts, but my favorite by far was our rather spontaneous trip to a silk factory and showroom.  We were greeted by a friendly young Chinese woman who asked if we were just there to shop or if we wanted to see the museum part of the factory that demonstrated how silk was made.  We quickly said we’d like to see the museum, and even though it was really just a two-room exhibit, the tour blew me away.  To give a bit of context: growing up with a mother who is a weaver, knitter, cross-stitcher, and now an alpaca farmer, fabrics and fiber arts have always been a bit of a fascination to me, but I never knew how silk was made, aside from that it came from a silk worm.  In a mere ten minutes, my knowledge of and fascination with this material grew tenfold.  The first room of the museum showed the lifecycle of the silk worm, from tiny larvae to full-sized worm the size of a pinky finger to cocoon to large moth.  Petri dishes with real samples of each stage lined the wall next to a big graphical display.  However, the only really unique part of this lifecycle was the cocoon, because that’s where silk comes from.  The next room was where the mystery was revealed. 

It just so happens that the entire cocoon of a silk worm is made of one continuous micro-thin, ultra-strong strand of silk, starting on the outside and ending on the inside where the worm becomes a moth.  It is possible, therefore, to find one end of the strand on the cocoon’s exterior and simply unravel it like pulling on the end of a ball of string.  How this painstakingly tedious process could ever be replicated on an industrial scale, though, was still beyond me. 

It turns out the method is both ingenious and shockingly simple.  After the silk worm finishes its cocoon but before it becomes a moth, approximately ninety percent of the cocoons are harvested and placed in hot water.  The water kills the worm and loosens the cohesion of the silk strand, making it possible to find the end.  Once that is done, eight cocoons are placed in one tiny hot water bath at the bottom of a wooden spinning wheel that simultaneously spins the eight strands together to produce a thicker, continuous thread and pulls up on the strands, allowing the weight of the cocoons to keep tension on the line.  As the wheel turns above, the eight cocoons simply tumble in the water as they’re unraveled into nothing.  The machine in the demonstration room had a dozen such strands going at once, but it was not hard to imagine the same parallel lines of new silk thread stretching all the way across a large factory floor.

This process, of course, was invented thousands of years ago, and it is not an overstatement to say that it changed the world.  Silk was the latest Apple iProduct of its day, that special thing everyone with money wanted but couldn’t make him or herself, and its day lasted over a millennium.  The silk trade spurred international commerce, attached its name to the first major link between East and West, and through the writings of Marco Polo and others cemented China as that wonderfully mysterious empire situated on the far side of the world.

This is not to say silk is no longer a lucrative trade today; to the contrary, silk still sells.  I have been offered silk scarves, bed covers, table clothes, shirts, ties and suits to buy just about everywhere I’ve been so far in China.  Apparently the silk business is booming among all range of consumers too, because these items range in price from very expensive to dirt cheap.  Of course, half of these items are also not real silk. 

In an ironic twist of modern commercialism, the same country that invented and prospered from silk, the finest fabric the world had ever known, has now perfected the art of imitating it, so much so that undiscerning tourists usually can’t tell the difference.  Or maybe they just don’t care.  The fact is, a part of modern tourism in China has become the ability to buy knock-offs of anything – designer handbags, watches, sunglasses, jeans – along with no-brand items of cheap make and material such as shoes, shirts, rugs, paintings or just about anything else you can imagine.  In the world of hyper-consumerism, cheap Chinese wares are the equalizer: your sunglasses can say Prada too for fifteen dollars, or ten if you’re a good haggler.  A local guide even told my friends and me that she calls Beijing’s famous pearl market the “Fake Market” because more fake stuff is sold there than real.

But what is fake, really, and what is real?  A watch that tells time doesn’t sound fake to me, but it also doesn’t mean it’s a Breitling.  The same applies to a handbag with Louis Vuitton written on the side of it.  But what about fake pearls?  Like silk, this deception is more than just false branding.  Yet if a scarf keeps you warm or a necklace gets you compliments, does it matter if it comes from a silk worm, an oyster or just a highly processed vat of plastic?  This sort of deception seems benign enough to me, but I also wonder if it erodes more than just the exclusivity of certain brands or materials.  Does it also erode our integrity?

On my second day in Beijing my friends and I went to the Great Wall.  Of course, what makes the Great Wall so great is the fact that it stretches over six thousand kilometers from the ocean in the east to the far end of ancient China in the west.  Understandably given its incredible length, there are many different locations at which a tourist can go see the wall.  We chose a relatively new site to be opened for visitors, a section of the wall straddling a mountain pass just an hour’s drive north of the capital.  When we got there I was instantly surprised by how well preserved this section of the wall was: all of the towers were in tact along with the gig saw ramparts and steep stairs.  As we climbed from the parking area upward, however, things started to look a bit strange to me, considering that this wall was supposedly hundreds of years old.  For one, from a higher vantage point one could easily see that the four-lane highway running perpendicular to the wall didn't pass over or under it, but neatly through perfectly sized arches made with stonework identical to that which we were climbing.  Secondly, the wall climbing up the ridge on the far side of the road disappeared half way up the distant hillside, perhaps ducking into another drainage, but nonetheless very different looking than the endless ridge top towers I had seen in so many pictures.  Finally, on the far side of the highest tower at the top of a very steep climb that only a small percentage of tourists reached, the wall abruptly stopped.  Yes, it just stopped!  I couldn’t believe it.  It was clear before then that extensive restoration of this part of the wall had been done before it was opened to tourists, but without even the ruins of an unrestored wall to trace before me to the horizon I began to wonder if there was any integrity in this site at all. 

The idea of the Chinese government creating an entirely fake section of the Great Wall just for tourists would have disturbed me more had I not been forewarned of just this sort of thing by my friend whose family has worked in and concerning China for two generations.  Long embracing a culture of visual propaganda, the Chinese government became obsessed with presenting the perfect image of China to the world for the 2008 Olympic Games, and I am willing to bet that no matter what fortification historically existed on this site, its proximity to the city made it a prime target for government-sponsored beatification, and that its recent opening as a tourist destination coincided with the buildup to the Olympics.  Nevertheless, I kept mostly silent about my disgust for this fake wall while my friends were still here.  There was no point in spoiling other people’s Great Wall experience based solely on a strong hunch.  After all, maybe all of the unrestored sections of the wall had simply disintegrated into the hills they once covered. 

However, three days later my suspicions were vindicated.  After my friends flew back to the United States, I joined a group tour with whom I will travel as far as Hong Kong, and our second day’s activity was to travel to a remote section of the Great Wall three hours from Beijing and trek several miles along it.  It only took minutes to understand upon seeing it that this was the real Great Wall.  Similar to the site I had visited earlier, evidence of heavy restoration existed near the entrance point.  However, the farther we walked, the less and less restored the wall became and the more and more authentic it looked given its age.  First most of the ramparts disappeared, and in their place the wall simply stopped like a cliff edge at either side.  Then the towers that dot the path of the wall every couple hundred yards and originally had second levels for lookouts became roofless shells of decaying walls and rubble.  Finally even the neatly placed steps and surface stones, perfectly erected in the restored sections, became piles of rubble with patches of grass growing from parts of them.  This was the real wall, believably hundreds of years old, and even just before I stopped to turn around after hiking on it for several hours, when I looked forward I could easily see the wall stretched along the hillcrest, dotted with decaying tours like a line of silent sentries, all the way to the far off horizon. 

So why make fake things?  Why lie?  Why substitute a genuine experience for a fabricated one?  This is the biggest question I have for China.  After my first visit to the “Great Wall” I left feeling disappointed and confused, and certainly without any feeling of having witnessed greatness. I even remember thinking to myself, “Wow, the Great Wall is nothing compared to the Taj Mahal or Machu Picchu.”  However, after my second visit, after seeing the real wall and walking along the remnants of the greatest military fortification and public works project in human history, I did appreciate the wall’s greatness and with it the people whose ancestors had built it.  For me, at least, deception begets weakness and honesty begets greatness. 

However, as I rode the bus back to Beijing the next day and saw the outskirts of this huge city grow up around me I realized that perhaps my definition of real is just a point of view.  Stretching alongside the highway I saw dozens of new housing developments whose McMansion architecture and scale could have been pulled out of any affluent suburb in America.  Yet those houses, complete with patios and two car garages, are homes to real Chinese families.  And within those houses, and houses just like them all over the world there are photographs from family vacations, maybe even to the Great Wall of China.  Was that vacation not real just because the wall everyone is sanding on is simply a replica of a historic building and not the real thing?  Are the silk pajamas containing only ten percent silk or plastic pearls with dust adhered to them to give that slightly rough pearl feel any less of a gift if it is just the thought that counts? 

I may be in a minority, but I say there is a difference, and there is a loss of value whether the ultimate recipient can recognize it or not.  Because every time we dilute something, every time we say something is real when it is in fact fake, we lose the ability to witness and understand true greatness.  Silk is great, the Great Wall is great, and China has been and can be great too.  Yet somehow, somewhere, someone decided instead to make China the land of fake things that can be consumed cheaply.  Be the silk factory, China, be the inventors of paper and rockets.  Don’t be the pretty girl made ugly by wearing too much makeup.
