The Trail Less Traveled

The past week has taken me from one of the most famous and touristy destinations in the world to an unsung and unnoticed community whose simple lifestyle surprisingly left me with an even greater impression. 

In the first case I am of course referring to Machu Picchu, and to take nothing away from it, there is good reason it is the coveted destination of travelers the world over.  Machu Picchu is an immaculately restored ancient Inca city situated high on a ridge in the Peruvian cloud forest thousands of feet above the winding Urubamba canyon.  Situated on such a narrow patch of land and surrounded alternately by steeply descending agricultural terraces, sharply rising sugarloaf peaks, and a nearly constant ring of mist and cloud rising from the valley below, Machu Picchu can only be described as a magical sort of place. 

This was actually my second visit to Machu Picchu, my first being almost a decade ago and the inspiration for my coming back to hike to the ancient city via the four-day trek along the equally ancient Inca Trail.  However, in an unplanned turn of events, a serious lung infection waylaid my hiking plans, and after a few days of forced recuperation in Cusco I was given no other option than to retrace the more conventional approach via train and bus.  This was my first disappointment. 

Although the site itself has lost none of its awe or wonder over the last ten years, this visit to Machu Picchu was markedly muted compared to my first, and upon reflection, the main reason is that Machu Picchu has become one of the world’s biggest tourist traps, and for the better part of two days I felt trapped in it.  Located literally a half-day’s train ride from the nearest through road, Aguas Calientes, more commonly called Machu Picchu Town, is a modern day boomtown with only one purpose: to house, feed, entertain and transport tourists (upwards of 2,500 every day) the thirty-minute bus ride from its location at the valley floor to the ruins of Machu Picchu on the ridge high above.  Sadly, in sharp contrast to the ruins, nothing in the town feels the least bit authentic.  Whereas ten years ago I visited a still touristy frontier town with poorly constructed two-story buildings, dirt roads and a healthy population of stray dogs, today it is completely different.  Its new brick-paved streets are lined with four-story hotels, internet cafes, and seemingly more pizzerias than Lower Manhattan.  And while the prevailing architecture is more that of cinderblock and brightly painted Inca-themed signs than the fake fronts of a Hollywood western set, the insincerity felt just the same.  That, combined with the over amplified panpipe band that made the rounds both lunches I spent there and the ubiquitous t-shirt shops displaying tourist favorites Inca Kola and Llama Sutra (yes, that is exactly what you think it is), made the entire place feel like a circus.  More than ever Machu Picchu has become an industry of pre-packaged memories, and business is booming.  Unfortunately, this barrage of commercialism, which lasts every minute spent at Machu Picchu not actually within the confines of the archeological site, has also robbed the place of much of what made Machu Picchu so amazing in the first place… that of all possible places, the Incas had built this incredible city in the most remote, beautiful, and perfectly isolated location conceivable.

From Machu Picchu my group and I returned to the city of Cusco, and from there set out via public bus to Lake Titicaca, an enormous inland sea on the boarder of Peru and Bolivia, and the highest navigable waterway in the world.  The main lakeside city of Puno is a dusty, dirty feeling place with a feeling of being situated at a forgotten end of the world.  The charm of Lake Titicaca, however, is what lies beyond Puno. 

On our first day there we boarded a thirty-foot motorboat and after a few words from our local guide set out straight for the middle of the lake.  Our destination: the remote community of Taquile Island.  As we got farther from Puno and closer to the island in front of us, all sense of time and place seemed to fade away.  The scenery looked practically Mediterranean as hilly grass-covered spits of land jutting out toward us adorned with stone fences and simple mud-brick houses.  Tiny rowboats and primitive looking sailboats passed us in the opposite direction, one even carrying two cows along with its passengers.  I imagined I was in Greece two hundred years ago.  More than anything, this new and unfamiliar landscape looked genuine and unspoiled. 

After reaching the island we were greeted briefly by a village elder, and then we walked the better part of a mile up the main stone path to the village center.  No vehicles exist on this island, and the narrow path barely gave room for cows and tourists to pass each other.  And although several women had blankets covered with woven handicrafts for sale as we walked past, the majority of the villagers appeared indifferent to our presence.  Lunch was grilled lake trout with a stunning view from the higher elevation of the village.  The lake stretched out almost to the horizon in every direction - Peru was nothing more than a distant mountain range to the left and Bolivia was nothing more than a distant mountain range to the right.  Our guide told us about the local culture, which had existed in this relative vacuum for centuries and still maintained much of its own language, customs and dress.  I loved every minute I was on that island, and I was sad to descend, after only an hour in the village center, the couple hundred stone steps to the harbor and our waiting boat below.

Already slightly nostalgic for my too-quick visit to Taquile Island, I had no idea that my group’s next stop would be even more impressionable.  We docked after an hour’s boat ride at the narrow stone pier of Luquina Chico, a small Aymara village on a remote peninsula on the Peruvian side of the lake.  Far from any noteworthy geographic or archeological site, the purpose of this visit was the simplest and at the same time the most unique of my entire journey in Peru – to interact with the local population.  Instead of staying at a hotel – there was no hotel – each of the sixteen people in my group would be paired off to spend the night and the following morning as the guest of a local family.

The homestay experience began at the village center: a cement soccer/basketball court at what appeared to be the only public building around.  My group fielded a team of six to play a friendly soccer match against the village boys.  Following this, a representative of each of our host families dressed us in traditional Aymara clothing – men in bright ponchos and women in multiple long skirts and bowler hats – and then we all learned one of their traditional dances.  As awkward or cheesy as this could have been, what made the experience so relaxing and enjoyable was that to the villagers our presence genuinely appeared to be more than a way to earn some extra money.  To them, these weekly day-long influxes of tourists appeared to be a welcome and amusing break from the ordinary, and watching us attempt their traditional dances was a genuine cultural exchange.

Following dancing the gathering broke up and we each followed our host family member, in my case the seventeen-year-old daughter, back to their respective houses for dinner.  On arrival I met the parents, Jesus and Natalie, and their second daughter of fifteen.  Dinner was simple (rice with potatoes and vegetables) and conversation simpler, but there was rarely awkwardness in the frequent silence.  Spanish was clearly their second language, Aymara being their first, but we could still share basic ideas with each other.  Bedtime came early for me and my German companion Andreas, as there was simply nothing else to do after finishing the meal. 

The next morning we were awoken by a polite knock on the door by our host father, and after a simple breakfast we joined him for his morning routine.  A subsistence farmer like everyone else in the village, he spent his time caring for his dozen farm animals (four cows, a donkey, a pig with several piglets, and two chickens), and tending the tiny hillside fields next to his house.  After we helped to move the pigs down the hill closer the lake, he asked if we would like to go out into the lake on his wooden boat, an offer Andreas and I quickly accepted.  We were soon a hundred yards off the shore looking back at the village and surrounding fields spread across the quickly rising slope before us, and it was here that a profound thought, the kind that can only sneak up upon someone when they least expect it, struck me.

Trying not to dwell on the fact that I was now in a small boat on an inland sea with a man named Jesus, I could not help thinking as I took a turn rowing the two cumbersome oars along the reed-lined shore that even though his and my lives could not be less similar and still intersect, he and I are both essentially following the life paths laid out before us.  He descends from generations of Aymara people who have farmed and fished these same places since before the Inca Empire was even formed, a fact nonchalantly confirmed when he laughed and answered “Hasta siempre” [since forever] to my question of how long his family had been living in this village.  And while my life may appear much more complex on the surface, I too am essentially following in the footsteps of my forefathers who for generations have been professionals, managers and even world travelers.  So while it would be easy to contrast the life opportunities afforded to both of us, the question I kept asking myself in that tiny boat on the edge of this huge body of water was did I choose my lifestyle and profession any more than he chose his? 

The night before, just before going to bed, I had walked forward a few paces in front of the mud-brick courtyard wall of my homestay’s residence to take in the amazing moonlit view of Lake Titicaca before me.  Jesus walked out after me, probably wondering if I had a question, but when I said nothing he stood there silently too.  Finally I asked him, “Is that Puno?” pointing to the only concentration of artificial lights that could be seen to the left far across the lake on a hillside near the horizon.  “Yes, that is Puno.”  There was no sense of envy in his gaze or his simple response.  Puno, with its loud streets and crowded marketplaces, though only a three-hour bus ride away, is a different world from where we were standing, as is, I thought to myself, New York City, where I spent my last day before departing the United States, a different world from that of Puno.

I pondered this realization heavily as I turned back toward the darkness of the lake in front of me, and I continued to think about it at length over the course of the next day.  While the next years of my life will almost certainly put me in the midst of that frantic self-absorption of a modern metropolis like New York, I hope deeply that I can remember this moment and this man who showed me a snapshot of his life, one where reading an analog clock is an unnecessary skill (he shyly requested that I read the hands on my watch when I unconsciously showed him my wrist after he asked for the time), but intricately anchoring a boat in the shallow lakeshore reeds is second nature.  I hope even more that when I find my life scheduled from before sunrise to after sunset, as again I assume it will eventually be, I can remember and appreciate that while we would both be poor contributors in the worlds each other inhabit, both I and Jesus are empowered to be masters within our own.

The next day I departed Luquina Chico for Puno, and the following day I traveled overland to La Paz, Bolivia.  And as I watched mile after mile of arid landscape, small villages, and modest farmsteads unfold before me through the bus window, I also wondered how many other lessons hide among them, yet to be discovered. 

December 10, 2011
