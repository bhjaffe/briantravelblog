The Unexpected Gifts of Travel

A Crossing of the Mind »

After an amazing month in Australia I once again find myself nestled into a window seat of a packed airliner flying high over a mixture of land and ocean below me.  My tour of Australia, in which I came to know two amazing cities, crossed the vast openness of the Outback, and explored the beauty and majesty of the Great Barrier Reef and the North Queensland Coast, was exactly what I needed at exactly the right time.  After the complete sensory overload of India, Australia just felt like an elongated traditional vacation: relaxing, exciting, scenic, but also easy.  In fact, my only regret flying away from Australia is that this amazing continent’s remoteness will keep me from finding casual excuses to return as frequently as I would wish to do so.  Even on my last day, as I walked with my friends along the much-recommended and truly breathtaking path from Coogee to Bondi Beaches in Sydney, I once again had to stop and ask why I would ever leave this place now that I had discovered it. 

The truth is, however, I am more excited than ever to go home, and the reason is that I finally know what it is I intend to go home to.  I’ve spent much of the last six months since leaving my previous life and employment trying to figure out what should be the next chapter of my life.  From arranging fact-finding phone calls in Ecuador to completing graduate school applications in Dubai to discussing pros and cons of business schools or consulting jobs with an old college friend in Mumbai to impatiently waiting for admission decisions in southern India, the question “What is next?” has consumed so much of my thought and reflection for a very long time.  But finally, after all of this thinking, discussing, waiting and rethinking, I have reached a decision, and I could not feel better about it: I have decided to move to Santa Barbara, California and to start a manufacturing company, which ironically enough is the same thing I told people I intended to do six months ago when I left my previous job, though at that time it just happened to be the leading contender among so many possible ideas.

So what allowed me to come to this decision and what makes me so happy about it?  I know there must be many factors, but one indisputable cause has simply been spending such a significant amount of time away.  Yes, in many ways it took getting far, far away from both my pervious life and all the other distractions that surrounded it to see both myself and my many options for the future in a better light.  Also, as someone heavily influenced by the opinions of those I am closest to, it has been invaluable to spend large periods of time alone or with only one or two close friends.  The result has been a steadily growing understanding of what I really want alongside a steadily growing confidence in my own ability to succeed at whatever it is I choose to pursue.  Whereas six months ago I knew I wanted to start a company eventually but hadn’t the courage to actually take the plunge, I now feel quite the opposite, and I can only deduce that some unforeseen consequence of all the experiences, adventures, challenges and tribulations that I have exposed myself to between then and now has been this new sense of confidence I have in myself. 

The funny thing about it, though, is that I didn’t believe this sort of change was possible as a result of travel alone, and I  even went so far as to dismiss the search for it through travel by others.  So many people I have encountered while traveling, be they backpackers in South America or hippies in India or year-long work visa holders in Australia, left home to “find themselves” and to “find their calling” in the world.  This was never my intention, and I viewed their quests as being as likely to succeed as a Hail Mary pass in football.  In fact, I originally never intended to travel for so long – my plan grew from a mere three-week vacation to India to coincide with my sister’s wedding reception in January.  However, when I spoke with friends about my plan to take some time between jobs to travel they wisely pointed out that I would never again have this amount of freedom for long-duration travel, so I began adding destinations and recruiting several of these same friends to join me for different legs.  Duration was simply a matter of pragmatism, and likewise the goals of my travel were totally external and in my mind pragmatic as well: to discover something about the world by taking a series of long exposure looks at different corners of it.  I never intended for the lens to point inward, and I certainly never expected to discover as much about myself as the places I would visit.

I suppose in this way I was the naïve one, because even in the short history of my own life I should have recognized the inherent personal value of getting away and seeing the world from a different angle.  When I was seventeen I studied abroad for a semester in a small town in Normandy, France, and ever since I have looked back on that experience as my intellectual renaissance and the time when I started to think of myself as an adult.  Well, I cannot say exactly how I will look back on this period of travel, but with the still-blurry perspective of being right next to it I can already tell it will be something similar to that of eleven years ago. 

After nearly six months of travel through South America, India and Australia I feel so good about what I have done and all the unexpected gifts I have taken away from this journey.  It is the way everyone should hope to feel at the end of such a long trip, and thus I am so thankful to be feeling it now.  In a few hours my airplane will land and the next chapter will begin.  But of course, by this I do not mean the next chapter, the one I’ve been obsessing over for so long.  What I mean is China!  Yes, amazingly I still have one last great adventure in front of me on this journey, and I can only guess what more gifts I will find there.

April 1, 2012
