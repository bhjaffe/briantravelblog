Glitz and Glamour

The Darkest Dark and the Lightest Light: Part II »

The past ten days have taken me from the natural majesty of the Andes Mountains and the modesty and humility of the people who live among them to the pinnacle of human-made grandness and the corresponding celebration of luxury and excess that appears to go hand in hand with it.  I am in Dubai, and out my hotel window towers the world’s tallest building in all its unapologetic glory.

Far be it from me to judge excess too harshly, however; in the past ten days I have flown enough distance to nearly circle the world.  I ended my South American adventure and flew home for Christmas from the cosmopolitan city of Buenos Aires, a place that until yesterday I would have described as a breath of fresh modernity into a region that often feels at the same time charmingly and frustratingly stuck in the last century.  However, Buenos Aires is an aged beauty compared to the debutante that is Dubai.  Nothing in this city of glass towers looks more than a decade old, and unlike Buenos Aires, nothing has the sophistication or prominence of having stood any test of time.  Dubai is like a land without history floating somewhere between the desert and the sea.  It is the true modern oasis, and even though the buildings and fountains and new eight lane freeways look real enough, like a desert mirage I also wonder if any of it would actually be there were I to break the trance it has surely set over me and try to reach out and touch it with my bare hands. 

But the trance is strong, and I would be lying if I said I was not enjoying it nearly as much as the next person.  Even before touching down at the Dubai airport after my sixteen-hour flight from Los Angeles the pomp and self-proud nature of this place was already on display.  Emirate Airlines, yes that same company that showed up sponsoring premier European soccer teams over a decade ago, has done all it can to promote itself as the classiest new way to gallivant around the globe.  In an obvious attempt to recreate the PanAm stewardesses of yesteryear, Emirates leaves no doubt as to the chief hiring criterion for their multicultural cadre of women flight attendants who parade their distinctive tan and red outfits (complete with what has to be the most bizarre hat of any professional uniform) around airport terminals and down the aisles of their brand new airplanes.  Wooden inlays in business class seats and windows?  Check.  The largest in-flight entertainment suite of any airline I’ve flown?  Check.  Any reason to question why you chose to fly Emirates or stop over in Dubai on your way to India?  None.

Aside from my father’s luggage having been lost by the airline somewhere at LAX (nobody’s perfect, I suppose), the pampering only continued once we arrived.  Our sixty-three-story five-star hotel is perfectly situated at the far end of an artificial decorative pond from the Burj al Khalifa, the world’s tallest building, and just to the side of the pond, which also features the world’s largest dancing fountain that shoots columns of water choreographed to music over one hundred feet into the air, lies the world’s largest and most luxurious shopping mall.  Seemingly every brand you’ve ever heard of has a store in this mall to include all of the most prestigious fashion houses and jewelry designers in the world.  As you stroll down the gigantic marble tiled “Fashion Wing” somewhere between Yves Saint Laurent and Bulgari, the sensation is actually more that of walking through a museum than a mall.  It is a museum, really – a museum of consumption – and on the weekend of New Years attendance is booming. 

Despite all its glitz and glamour, however, there is another reality in Dubai that is impossible for me to ignore: that only the richest among us can ever come here and behave in this way, buying expensive Persian rugs and jeweled watches, eating multiple course meals at fancy waterside restaurants beneath towering skyscrapers, and staying in lavishly decorated hotel suites.  And even though there is seemingly no lack of demand for these opportunities, especially on this of all weekends (reservations for nearly everything have been booked for weeks in advance), I have come to no conclusion that the incredible expense associated with being here delivers, beyond its exclusivity, any lasting value greater than a stroll through the dusty streets of San Pedro de Atacama, another desert tourist town located just over the Bolivian boarder in Chile, and where I was only two weeks ago.  Looking at the faces of the other tourists in the bars, restaurants, clothing boutiques or even while watching the incredible dancing fountains here reveals no unique bliss reserved only for the affluent.  Their faces look the same as any other crowd, but oh, how much more they – we – have all paid to be part of this crowd. 

I will certainly enjoy New Years Eve tonight – we have reserved a table at a waterfront restaurant with stunning views of the fountains and the Burj al Khalifa after all.  I’m equally sure that watching the world’s tallest building light up with fireworks at midnight from my twentieth story balcony will be a sight I’ll never forget.  But tomorrow I will also go back to the Dubai airport and board an airplane to India where I hope with renewed vigor that during my two month tour I will have an impressionable experience of a deeper kind.  To me the greatest joy of travel is exploration, and the greatest exploration is seeing and experiencing that which is unknown and unexpected, not that which is simply hard to achieve.  While the crystal palace where I watch the sun rise today is undeniably exclusive, there is nothing altogether special about it to me.  Special is something much greater than this, and it is also where I hope to watch the sun set tomorrow.
