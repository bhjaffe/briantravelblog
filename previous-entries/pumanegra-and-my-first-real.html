PumaNegra and My First Real Ride

« Fiestas de Cuenca
Previous Entries
Team Swiss »

I'm on the road at the edge of Cuenca, searching for a landmark to show me where I am.  PumaNegra, my so-named 200 cc motorcycle, rumbles beneath me better than she has ever sounded before.  This is my fourth outing, but today I am determined to do what I failed to accomplish any time before, which is get out of Cuenca entirely and explore the countryside beyond.

My ownership of PumaNegra, although only a week old, has already had its ups and downs.  From constant stalling when I first bought her to the crippling one-two combo of a flat tire plus a blocked fuel line last time I took her out (clogging a busy intersection, rolling her to the nearest mechanic), our relationship hasn't been the smoothest.  But today I am feeling confident - and adventurous.  I bought a motorcycle to explore the verdant mountainscape I saw from the airplane when I flew into Cuenca two weeks ago, and I'll be damned if a few hiccups will leave me too scared to leave the city limits and the lifelines therein.  

I finally spot the hospital I've been looking for and make a sharp right turn.  As I come over a rise I see my goal in front of me, an almost forgotten looking two-lane road leading straight into a gap in the ring of hills surrounding the city.  As I cross the last intersection and roll on the throttle a tinge of excitement runs through my body.  I feel like I'm escaping from something and where I'm going next is completely unknown.

Almost instantly, both my surroundings and my mood change.  With two rolling curves through the tree filled gap, I am officially out of Cuenca and into the countryside!  The road is smooth with very few other vehicles on it, and I instantly feel relief from being free of city traffic.  Old and new houses line the road and the farther I go the more distance there is between each structure.  The ground is dark green and brown and the smell of turned soil and farm animals fills the air.  Men and women walk the roadsides, and I pass the occasional public bus.  I am happy knowing that at least I won't be completely stranded if something goes wrong here.  

As I wind along the curvy road and rise farther into the hill country the landscape changes subtly again.  The farmhouses become less frequent and between the fields there are now patches of forest and untilled land.  I enter a small town, maybe better described as a village, with a central square and a prominent church towering above all other structure at more than twice their height.  This is where I make my first navigational decision.  The paved road goes left, but it appears to be heading downhill.  I want to keep going up, and to the right of the church a gravel road curls behind the village.  It is big enough to be a road that might actually go somewhere, so I decide to take it.  My feeling is instantly confirmed I see coming toward me a blue public bus lumbering over ruts and billowing a cloud of dust behind it.  

This road is definitely new territory for PumaNegra and me, and I am grateful for her high clearance and traction tires.  As I continue up the rut-filled road at a conservative twenty-five kilometers per hour on my speedometer, the surroundings become even more rural and the few people I pass look even more surprised to see me there.  Two children point and laugh excitedly as I drive by and two dark skinned women, one with a child on her back and another with long black hair below her waist slowly move out of the center of the road as I approach them from behind.  If I ever wondered whether I'd see the real Ecuador, I think I have found it.  I want to stop and take a picture of the impressive views off to my left, but I worry that to linger for any amount of time longer than it takes to drive by would forfeit the locals' forgiveness for my having disrupted their usual serenity.

I finally reach the highest point of the road.  Towering above me to my right is a jagged mountain with two peaks that look like giant triangular shark teeth coming upward out of the ridge.  It's not the mountain's height that is impressive, but how quickly and how closely I have approached it.  By contrast, to my left an entire valley stretching over lower hills almost all the way to the horizon.  Ominous looking clouds lurk overhead.  They're probably at 20,000 feet, but from this elevation the clouds look close too.  

As I descend I am now determined to find a place to stop and take a picture.  I pass through a grouping of houses and make a sharp turn away from the mountain.  A quick rise follows and then before me I see a flat patch of undeveloped land, so I stop my bike and get out to enjoy the view.

Taking my helmet and gloves off I walk along the little knoll sticking out into the valley's center.  The view is extraordinary!  I'm pretty sure I see Cuenca far off to the left, but I can't make out the distinctive blue domes of the Cathedral, so maybe I'm just seeing the suburbs.  To the right I see the highway running alongside the river at the bottom of the valley, and the giant hilltop statue (I think of Jesus, though I'm not sure) that towered above a town my bus drove through last weekend looks tiny and low in the distance.  This would be such a great place for a picnic.  I'll remember this spot if I ever have someone crazy enough to want to ride with me.  I snap a few pictures then remount my bike for the descent.

Almost immediately I remember why I enjoy going uphill so much more than going downhill.  Breaking becomes much more difficult, and I move slowly as each steep section seems to be directly followed by a ninety degree turn.  I come to another village, this one even smaller than the first, and as if to greet me in the middle of the road stand two cows with "we don't let just anyone past" expressions and mean looking horns to go with them.  And I'm closing fast!  Thankfully, they're tethered such that they can't reach the far left side of the road, and without waiting to see just how much interest they want to take in me, I scoot around them to safety.  Happily past the bulls, the village's second welcome committee catches me off guard: a very large barking dog.  He is definitely not tethered and is running straight at me!  I accelerate aggressively, and luckily for him and me we miss each other by a couple feet.

All of this animal avoidance has made me unaware that is has started to sprinkle.  And by sprinkle, the now constant pings on my helmet's face shield remind me, I do mean rain.  It's funny, because being so protected from the elements by my helmet, jacket and gloves, it almost takes looking down at my now wet pants to confirm this fact.  The deceptive thing is that the valley directly in front of me is still entirely soaked in sunlight, so perhaps I can just outrun this raincloud.

The puddle filled dirt road finally changed back to asphalt and straightens out considerably.  With this improvement in traction and line of sight, I zoom downhill at my fastest pace yet.  I leave the rain and almost instantly I am dry again.  I am reentering modernity.  Dump trucks pass me going up the hill and new looking building begin to dot the roadside.  Finally, as if to confirm that my overland trek has ended as quickly as it began, in front of me are the highway underpass and my ticket back to Cuenca.  

The ride back on the highway is easy and uneventful.  My only real anxiety comes from flashbacks of the last time I traveled such a road on a two-wheeled vehicle, but that was at night in Tuscany, Italy on a 100 cc scooter with an equally terrified girlfriend clinging to me from the nub of a backseat she was sitting on - in other words, that was completely different.

After exiting the highway an off-ramp early and then making a couple requisite wrong turns, I triumphantly arrive back at my host family's home. What an amazing ride, and to think how easy it was too!  As the sun goes down I look at the mountains surrounding the city just a little differently now.  Your secrets aren't as safe as they once were.  I'm coming to see them, just give me a few days.  

October 29, 2011
