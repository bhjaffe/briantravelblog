The Darkest Dark and the Lightest Light: Part I »

I have seen the devil and I have walked on clouds, and all I had to do was go to Bolivia.

Part I: The Dark

Part II: The Light

Not very often do you find yourself in a place so unique that not only you never expected to be there, but furthermore you never though it could exist in the first place.  However, that is precisely where I found myself this week in the high desert of Bolivia.  Granted, I had assumed before arriving that the Uyuni Salt Flat would be an amazing place to visit after having browsed pictures of it on the internet when I signed up for my trip a few months ago, but even with this high level of anticipation, the degree of awe it inspired within me superseded even my wildest imagination.

The salt flat is located west of the Bolivian frontier town of Uyuni, in the southwest corner of the country.  I use the description frontier deliberately because not only is this desert town the historic railroad depot for Bolivian trains headed for ports on the Chilean coast, its sprawling layout also suggests that space in this arid valley is of absolutely no concern, and that like it or not, this is the last patch of civilization before leaving the world of human influence and entering another one altogether.  

That is what the town says to the discerning traveler as he walks its wide dusty streets and long gridded blocks.  However, to an English-speaking tourist, of whom there is a seeming endless stream, the town's message is much simpler: "The Salt Flat is Here!"  Even though the flatness of the valley makes seeing this enormous nearby geographic feature impossible, it is similarly impossible not to know that you're at its doorstep.  Every third storefront has pictures of it, either to advertise its own tour service or to simply grab your attention for another reason.  Bars and restaurants catering to tourists are lined with humorous optical illusion photographs such as people fighting plastic dinosaurs or eating their friends made possible by the salt flat’s perfectly flat white surface that stretches to the horizon.  And if all this were not enough, the last unavoidable indication of the nearby roadless expanse is that every other car moving through the Uyuni streets is a Toyota Land Cruiser with a spare fuel drum and a pile of tarp covered luggage tied to the roof rack. 

So with all this buildup, it is suffice to say that on the morning my group met our own trio of Land Cruisers we were pretty excited for the upcoming journey.  Our first stop, however, had nothing to do with the salt flats.  We drove a mile or so into the desert on the far side of town and stopped at Uyuni’s other major tourist attraction: the Train Graveyard. Exactly what it sounds like, this set of three parallel tracks houses the carcasses of a once-proud fleet of steam engines that used to transport Bolivian products and imported goods to and from the sea until prohibited in the nineteen nineties by a diplomatic rift with Chile.  Their rusted iron bodies are now nothing more than a jungle gym for tourists who climb over them with the glee and abandon of little children unleashed on a new and unsupervised playground.  Photos, the first of literally thousands to be taken over the next days, were snapped en masse.  However, as fun as this side show was, the main event still lingered somewhere out there in the desert, and I for one began wondering if it would ever live up to the anticipation. 

A half hour later we made yet another stop billed as our last toilet break, and we mulled for fifteen minutes at this outpost of roadside nick-knack vendors knowing that we were only minutes from the destination some of us had been waiting the entire trip to reach.  Finally we loaded back into our vehicles and were finally en route directly to the salt flats.  Then, in essentially the same amount of time it took to process this thought, we rounded a corner, rolled down the slightest decline, and drove out onto the endless white opening up before us.  We were there!

The salt flats were instantly one of the most unique things I had ever seen, and we had just started our crossing.  The ground was almost pure white and to look out the car window was to be at the same time blinded and awe struck.  The first mile or so nearest to Uyuni has been set aside for industrial salt production, so our first stop was actually amongst the thousands of meter-high salt mounds that have been formed by local workers in the extraction process.  Immediately upon stepping out of the vehicle I reached down and grabbed a huge handful of the white paste at a shallow puddle of standing water.  The salt was shockingly smooth and felt like it was seeping into my hands like mud, but it also left a chalky white residue after the remainder of what I let slip through my fingers quickly dried.  Not too far away we could still see the light sand and sagebrush of the desert and town beyond.  I did not realize at the time that this would be our last view of civilization for three days.

Back in the Land Cruiser excitement was overwhelming.  Our driver turned up his Spanish pop mix to high volume and we raced over the expansive white in the direction of the dry lake’s center.  This is when we began to realize just how much of a treat we were in for.  As would be expected given its location next to the world’s driest desert, for most of the year the main body of the Uyuni Salt Flat is completely devoid of surface water.  However, due to an unseasonably strong rainstorm the night before, the majority of the lake (over 4000 square miles) had actually filled with anywhere from half an inch to two inches of water, giving testament to the fact that this surface really is one of the flattest places on Earth.  But more importantly, the rain was the cause of our extraordinary good fortune: the entire salt flat had turned into a giant mirror, and we were only beginning to understand the implications of what that meant.

It took half an hour for the effect to become full because the center of the lake was a few inches below the outer rim and therefore more completely covered in water, but once we were sufficiently in the middle the surrealism of the place took over.  What had once been a dark blue sky with puffy white clouds above became a dark blue sky with puffy white clouds below as well.  As we got out of the Land Cruiser and stepping off the tiny dry patch where we had parked, the sensation was nothing short of walking on top of clouds.  With nothing attached to the ground upon which to focus your eyes, distance became irrelevant.  People and vehicles simply floated by in various sizes.  A Hollywood director could not create a better set for heaven if he tried. 

We drove on, stopping at intervals, farther and farther onto the salt flat.  Yet even after a lunch stop at a spectacular cactus covered island in the lake’s center and another stop on a nearby dry patch of salt to try our own hands at the wild optical illusion photographs we’d seen before arriving, that spectacular mirror effect was by far the most impressionable for all of us.  The unexpected presence of the water on the salt flat’s surface had forced our drivers to stick to more cautious speeds, which meant what normally would have been a three hour crossing was now taking six hours, but none of my group minded.  As the sun got low in the sky our trio of Land Cruisers was still driving across that seemingly endless mirror, and the sunset that followed, with the triangle shaped mountains on the horizon identically displayed both above and below the distant edge of the salt flat, is one I will never forget. 

That night we stayed in a salt hotel, a structure where literally everything – walls, beds, tables – is made of hardened salt bricks, and the following two days we drove through an incredible roadless expanse of volcano filled desert with  breathtaking vistas, amazing rock formation, geysers, and flamingo filled lagoons.  But to me, and as shocking as it is to say considering the incredible sights of those following days, none of it compared to the afternoon I spent on the salt flat. 

That sensation of driving, walking, running, crouching and even just standing on top of what looked by every measure to be a perfect copy of the sky above was a feeling so out of this would it is still hard to imagine that it actually happened on it.  To be sandwiched in that lightest light, of the clouds above, the reflection below, and the salt flat that stretched from just below my feet to the distant horizon, was to be alone for a brief moment in space and time, plucked from reality, in naked awe of what world I live in.  And while I may never feel quite that same sensation again in this life, I could not help wondering as I took it all in if this were not the most perfect visualization of the next.
