Cotopaxi

« Travel Time Out

It is 6:30 am.  After an all night climb to the top of the world’s highest active volcano, an incredible sense of triumph fills the thin air.  A world apart – this patch of earth towering 5,897 meters (19,347 feet) above sea level is covered by nothing but the deepest blue of a high altitude sky and affords views of over a hundred miles in every direction that only an airplane pilot or a high-flying Andean condor could otherwise capture.  As you take in the panorama of mountains, valleys and other snow-capped volcanoes in the distance, the feeling can only be described as feeling like you’re on top of the world. 

Cotopaxi - 24 hours after I was on top

Or at least, that must be what the people on top of the mountain right now are feeling.  I was standing there – at the top of the mountain that now seems to be staring directly at me through perfectly clear morning air – exactly twenty-four hours ago, but my feeling was nothing like what I had imagined it would be.

Twenty-four hours ago the entire mountain was engulfed by clouds, and for every minute of the seven hours I climbed – five hours up and two hours down – snow fell unrelenting from the heavens, allowing no more than a hundred yards of visibility at the very best of times.  Why even attempt to climb such a high mountain in these conditions?  It’s a fair question, and most people, either through conjecture or exhaustion decided that reaching the summit was indeed a fool’s errand and turned around long before.  But for reasons that were a mix of stubborn determination, a vague sense of curiosity, and what turned out to be an empty hope that the clouds would break at higher elevations, I kept trudging along, one step after another, always upward, even when more than once the people I had been climbing with in a rope team turned around to go down.

My Cotopaxi climb actually started the day before around noon.  Simone, John and I were picked up at our hostel by our guides and issued crampons, ice axes, cold weather hiking boots, and any other gear we might need.  After about an hour drive into the national park and a little ways up the mountain, we began our hike to the refugio where we would spend the evening.  Although steep, the trail of volcanic gravel was straight and uneventful, and an hour later we were all inside the two-story cinder block structure that would be a temporary home to over thirty hikers and their guides that night.  An outing to the base of the glacier followed lunch, and we practiced using our crampons and ice axes.  To me, however, this was just a way to kill time.  I was excited and restless to start the real hike, but that wouldn’t begin for another seven hours.  As we returned to the refugio, clouds seemed to be getting closer to the mountain, an ominous sign of what we would awake to hours later.

Another meal, a quick meeting telling us which climbers would be going with which guides, and then began the long dark wait.  It was seven o’clock at night, and all the hikers had been directed to the bunked cots upstairs.  I’d heard about this part from those who had climbed before, and I wasn’t excited.  It was impossible to sleep.  Not only was the mix of nervousness and anticipation in the room thick enough to cut with a knife, the guides were all boisterously socializing directly below us, and there was no door to keep the noise from carrying up the stairs.  People would walk by on the loud creaking floor, others would noisily adjust their sleeping bags, still others would be coughing or even snoring if they’d somehow managed to fall asleep.  My mind wondered.  I thought about everything but the climb.  I just wanted to start, but I knew I had to get through this night first.  Finally, after literally hours of tossing and turning I fell asleep.  I dreamed of being in Japan and climbing Mt. Fuji, a strikingly similar looking mountain, although an entirely different sort of climbing experience from the one I was about to have.

Finally it was time.  After another quick meal and a lengthy process of making sure everyone had all the proper gear on, we set out from the refugio.  Stepping out the front door, we were met by a complete blizzard.  There were already three inches of new snow on the ground, and visibility was only a dozen yards.  Headlamps on and heads bent down only caring to view the next step in front of us, we marched single file off into the darkness.

Mine was one of the last groups to leave, and as the intensity of the snowfall waxed and waned, occasionally I could make out strings of lights high above me on the mountainside.  Otherwise my world had been shrunk to a size that extended forward from my boots only a yard or two.  Every time I lifted my leg for a new step upward, whatever substance had just been supporting it seemed to vanish behind me into nothingness.  The lack of any landmark or changing view created the surreal effect of moving in slow motion.  I kept walking forward, but nothing was changing around me except every five or ten minutes when we shifted from a diagonal upward path to the right to a diagonal upward path to the left. 

An hour into the hike things already seemed to be going poorly.  Taking a second look because at first I didn’t believe my eyes, high above my group a chain of lights was moving toward us, backward down the mountain.  It passed out of earshot, but soon another group went by, and this time my guide called over to them in rapid Spanish I couldn’t understand.  “What is the problem?” I asked.  “Too much snow,” was the response.  But we kept going up, so I guess we would make our own decision.  When we stopped at the base of the glacier to put on our crampons and rope up between climbers, my friend Simone, who I had known from language school in Cuenca and who was supposed to be my companion on this adventure, decided to turn back with another descending group because of a severely upset stomach.  We were only a fifth of the way up, but I was already alone with my guide in an every dwindling string of hikers still moving upward.

Up and up, seemingly forever, I silently followed in my guide’s footsteps.  Finally the featureless slope gave way to giant formations of ice-covered rock that we weaved our way through, still in the pitch black of night.  The snow continued, and now with walls of ice frequently towering above me, the sensation of not moving gave way to that of being underwater.  I felt like I was moving through a series of undersea caves, still silent and still with the narrowest field of lamp-lit vision in front of me.  Up until this point I felt physically quite strong, but when we were required to scamper up the occasional icefall on all fours with aggressive swings of our ice axes I felt the onset of high altitude fatigue.  I was now above 5,400 meters (17,700 feet) and way higher than I’d ever been before. 

We rounded the corner of a giant rock outcropping and in front of us stood another guide and his two hikers.  They were not moving.  A quick conversation in Spanish between the two guides was followed by a simple instruction: unhook from the rope and trade places with the guy in front of me.  Another hiker was going down.  At this point it became clear that only three hikers of the original thirty were still attempting to reach the summit.  Were we the three hardest or just the three dumbest?  I tried not to dwell on it.  Together with the two remaining guides, we continued onward and upward into the night.  Exhaustion got worse.  The pace slowed.  The young German exchange student Lorenzo in front of me in the rope line was sighing heavily.  I didn’t feel much better. 

Finally, as if someone had slowly turned a dimmer light switch, the dimensionless black faded into a dimensionless white.  Somewhere out there the sun was rising, but we couldn’t see a single ray of direct light.  Our guides assured us we were close.  Very close.  Only a hundred meters of elevation more, but oh, what a distance that was!  At this stage of the climb any mountain feature that required more than a simple step was exhausting to the point of requiring at least a minute long break.  Jumping a narrow crevasse?  Completely out of breath.  Scampering on all fours up a steep patch?  Completely out of breath.  Falling sideways due to the thick new snow now over a foot high?  Completely out of breath.  Lorenzo and I were spent, and every time a new higher contour of the mountain revealed itself from the whiteout in front of us we could not believe we hadn’t yet reached the summit. 

And then, just like that, we were there.  We had reached the summit.  There was no more mountain to climb.  The feeling was – indescribable - but not in a Hollywood ending sort of way.  It was some mix of relief, emptiness, and disappointment that after all that, we really wouldn’t get above the clouds and get a chance to experience first hand that view we had all seen pictures of.  Lorenzo fell to the ground, perhaps crying, though I wasn’t sure.  I just stood there, trying to process it all.  Our guides congratulated us.  We took some pictures.  The falling snow that had been our constant companion just drilled us from every direction.  My backpack was frozen shut. 

Me at the summit

The hike down was its own brand of misery, but at least we knew we were approaching the end.  The guides were worried that the snow accumulation would cover our tracks and make the trail difficult to follow, so they pushed the pace aggressively to the point that we were literally running through the heavy snow.  A patch that might have taken us half an hour on the way up we flew down in minutes, but the exhaustion was also overwhelming.  Being at the end of the rope line, any time I lost balance and couldn’t maintain the pace I would simply get pulled over face first into the snow by the rope and the momentum of the two people in front of me.  Several times either Lorenzo or I collapsed to the ground in protest.  Our guide understood and would give us a minute’s rest; he knew how tired we were, but he also knew that getting off the mountain was the only real solution.  Down, down, down we flew.  What I would have given for some skis!  Finally we reached the featureless slope where our climb had begun so many hours before.  And then, with the increased visibility at this lower elevation, we finally made out the yellow roof of the refugio and some human shaped figures in the distance.  We were done.

Now free of the leash that the climbing rope had become during the decent, I stumbled the last hundred yards to the refugio patio.  Sinking into the masonry wall I struggled to take off my crampons.  Simone came out the front door – she must have heard that we’d returned.  She congratulated me and chuckled at my clumsiness.  “Take a picture of me now… if you want… because you’ll never see me… in a more vulnerable state,” was all I said in return between panting breaths of air. 

Four hours and a brief nap later we were dropped off back at the hostel where I am now.  I received many congratulations, being the only hostel guest to have made it to the top.  I was happy to have completed what I had set out to do, but the euphoria that everyone expected was clearly not within me.

It wasn’t until today, perhaps at this very moment even, now that I’ve showered, shaved, and enjoyed a rejuvenating night’s sleep, that I can be content with yesterday’s hike up Cotopaxi.  I am happy I did it, and I am happy I made it to the top even though there was nothing to particularly see or experience up there.  Mine was a unique climb to be sure – like a journey deep into a science fiction novel and then a return journey back out of it.  And while I’ll probably never climb Cotopaxi again or get that amazing view or picture everyone dreams of, I got something equally if not more memorable, even if it is something that truly had to be felt to be understood.

November 25, 2011
