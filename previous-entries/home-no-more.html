Home No More

A Wide World Awaits »

It might seem antithetical to start a world tour by going home, but that's exactly what I did this past week.  It was really the first time I'd been home since college without a firm agenda or holiday to celebrate, and it was both refreshing and revealing.  By refreshing I mean that life was abundantly simple for me back at my parents' house: I woke up and went to bed whenever I wanted, I ate delicious home-cooked meals that I took no part in preparing, I always had a car to use, and both my parents were happy to meet me for lunch or take time off work to be with me whenever I desired it.  In other words, I was completely spoiled.  And that was great. 

The companion of that ease and comfort of home living, however, was a very raw sense of nostalgia, stronger and with greater permanence than during any past visit.  Perhaps that's precisely because I had no agenda this time.  My last few visits home, aside from being brief, were centered around either a major holiday, a surprise birthday celebration, or a wedding, all of which added new memories and experiences as well as provided my mind with ample distraction.  But this time I did relatively nothing.  Granted, I did do a great deal of packing, tying up of loose ends, and otherwise preparing for an extended journey, but all of these activities were centered around my impending travel, and all were quite forgettable in nature.  

So what was I left with this time as I either lounged around my parents' house or drove around doing errands in the city I grew up in?  Memories.  Memories of good times - high school, cross-country team friends and all the mayhem we made around our little corner of the city.  Memories of bad times - doctors offices and the too much time and worry I spent within their walls as a kid.  And then memories of a different type altogether - memories of girls.  Driving by the house of that girl from high school I never quite hit it off with or the places I'd been on dates with an ex-girlfriend, and how some of those nights ended.  All of these memories, some faded, but many still quite vivid, followed me around this past week, and they all seemed to be saying something to me: "You don't live here anymore."  Not in a spooky, ghostly sort of way, but nevertheless, with a strong, permanent expression that this is where I once lived, but not where I live any longer.  

So ironically, even as I visited banks, called credit card and cell phone companies, renewed my drivers license, rewrote my resume, and signed my taxes - all with either full or partial purpose to re-establish my address as that of my childhood - I felt the strongest sense yet that this is no longer my home.  

My home, I feel optimistically, is waiting out there somewhere, beyond the horizon, but I also feel, longingly, that I have much more to do before I find it.

October 14, 2011
